What Changed with the Sources

The original Sprint 2 recommendations were design-driven. The historical sources don't change the what — we still need economy rework, cart mechanics, settlement depth, event variety, and crew systems. But they transform the how and why of every recommendation. Here's what's different:

Before (Design-Only)

  • "Add cart squeal mechanic" — because it's a push-your-luck tradeoff
  • "Add settlement actions" — because stops need depth
  • "Add multi-step events" — because choices need variety
  • "Add crew personality" — because characters need attachment
  • "Balance economy" — because win rate is 0%

After (Source-Grounded)

  • "Cart squeal heard for miles" — Brehaut documents exactly how it works and why. It's not a design invention; it's a historical fact that becomes a mechanic.
  • "Sunday rest: mend trousers, grease wheels, sing hymns" — Marbot describes the actual weekly rhythm. Settlement actions emerge from what people actually did.
  • "The Geometry Lesson" — Marbot's mule literally tried to kill him. That's not a designed event; it's a story that becomes one.
  • "10 captains, 10 guides, flag system, penalties for theft" — Ross documents the actual governance structure of a cart brigade.
  • "1,089,000 lbs of meat from one hunt" — Ross gives us the real numbers for calibrating the food economy.
"When loaded it gave forth a blood-curdling squeal which could be heard for miles and which came to be associated with it. This squeal was caused by the friction of the dry wood of the hub turning on the axle and could not be eliminated. If grease was used the dust of the trail would mix with the grease and wear down the axle." — Brehaut (1972), after Alexander Henry's 1801 account

Sources Change the Priority

Some recommendations move up because the sources make them easier to implement with authenticity:

  • Cart mechanics jump to #1. Brehaut gives us exact specs (550 lb cart, 500 lb load, 5'3" wheels, 12 spokes, dished design, shaganappi repairs, wheel-raft river crossings). This isn't guessing — it's documenting.
  • Events get specific. Instead of "add multi-step events," we have actual scenarios: the Geometry Lesson (mule), the Mosquito Siege, the Prairie Fire, the Eagle Hunt, the Buffalo Hunt (1,210 carts, 1,089,000 lbs of meat). Each event writes itself from the source.
  • Settlement depth gets real. Ross describes the Sunday rest day in perfect detail. Marbot describes camp life, hymn singing, theology lectures on microscopes. The settlement isn't abstract anymore — it's a place.
  • Economy calibration gets anchored. Real numbers: 28-36 food needed for a trip, 500 lb cart capacity, 20-25 miles per day, 10-cart brigades. We can balance against reality instead of guessing.

New Recommendations from Sources

The sources also add recommendations that weren't in the original plan:

  • Cart voice / narrative perspective: The game should sound like someone who was there. Marbot's voice — wry, observant, self-deprecating — is the model.
  • Environmental rhythm: Day-by-day trail life with Sunday rest, seasonal changes, landscape milestones. The journey should have a rhythm, not just be "click travel."
  • River crossings as events: Brehaut documents multiple crossing methods (ford, cajeux/ferry, wheel-raft). Each river should be a decision point.
  • Brigade social structure: 10 carts, 3 drivers, overseer, flag system, council of captains, penalties for infractions. This is the social fabric of the trail.
Critical

Tight but Fair (from Ross)

Ross describes the 1840 hunt as a massive logistical operation. The game's economy should mirror this: the player is always close to the edge, but smart play gets them through. Ross's numbers prove the trail was survivable — thousands of people made it.

  • Target win rate: 30-40% for an informed player. Current: 0%.
  • Food should last ~28 days without resupply. At 1 food/day, 30 starting = 30 days. First settlement at day ~3. At day ~30, another settlement. Final push from Fort Edmonton at ~day 38.
  • Settlements are lifelines. +2 food at rest means the player who manages their stops right can stretch 30 food to 40+ days.

2. Cart System (Source-Grounded)

Brehaut gives us exact cart specifications. Marbot gives us the daily reality of cart travel. This isn't designed — it's documented.

Cart Specs (Brehaut)

  • Weight: 550 lbs (cart body)
  • Load capacity: 500 lbs
  • Wheel diameter: 5'3"
  • Body: 3' × 5-6'
  • Total length: 12'4"
  • Spokes: 12 (most common)
  • Wood: Elm hubs, oak axle/shafts, ash spokes/felloes
  • NO metal in construction

New Mechanics from Sources

  • Cart Squeal: "Blood-curdling squeal heard for miles." NWMP detection doubled.
  • Dished Wheels: 3-inch dish for stability. Prevents tipping on rough terrain.
  • Felloe Repair: Shaganappi + soaked rawhide binding. Temporary, lasts 1 segment.
  • Wheel-Raft Crossing: Remove wheels, lash concave-up, ferry across.
  • Sunday Grease: "Greased afresh their cart-wheels." Removes squeal for 3 segments.

3. Settlement Overhaul

Marbot describes Sunday rest in perfect detail. Ross describes the social fabric. The settlement isn't abstract — it's a place with rhythm and routine.

"Then our trousers and morals were mended, or, at least, patched up to appear a little better, the emigrants greased afresh their cart-wheels and their good resolutions, and washed away their sweat in the nearest river or lake. The man of science divided his time between Paul's Epistles and the compound microscope... and all of us ended the day generally by gathering about the camp-fire after supper and singing Old Hundred, Balerma, Dundee, Ward." — "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)

Sunday Rest Mechanic (from Marbot)

SystemMarbot
⛪ Sunday Rest Day
Every 7th day, crew can rest. Actions: mend clothes (-1 wear +1 morale), grease cart wheels (remove squeal), bathe in river (+1 morale), theology/science lecture (-1 boredom), evening hymn singing (+2 morale). Crew must stay rested or risk exhaustion.

5. Crew System

Ross documents the brigade's social structure. Dumont gives us the Métis voice — practical, skeptical, loyal. The crew isn't abstract anymore.

"Je n'ai pas d'instruction, c'est vrai, réplique celui-ci, mais j'ai en moi un gang que vous n'avez pas, ce qui vous empêche d'agir comme vous le devriez, vous, Métis. Vous pouvez remercier Mitchell, qui s'est toujours montré notre ami." — Dumont, in Ouimet (1885). "I have something you don't — a backbone."

Brigade Social Structure (from Ross)

  • Council of Captains: 10 captains elected for each brigade of 10 carts
  • Flag system: Camp flag = authority. When flying, the guide is chief. When lowered, captains govern camp.
  • Penalties for infraction: 1st offense: saddle and bridle cut up. 2nd: coat cut up. 3rd: flogging. Theft: name called 3× in center of camp + "thief"
  • Sunday gathering: "Old Hundred, Balerma, Dundee, Ward"
SystemRoss
👥 Crew Morale & Bonus System
Each crew member has: Health (Healthy→Injured→Critical→Dead), Morale (Good→Steady→Low→Breaking), Skills (+2 to relevant rolls). Loss of crew = loss of bonuses. Death = permanent. Brigade: join for shared security, reduced speed, social events.
4-5 hours
