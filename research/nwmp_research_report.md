## 1. NWMP Patrol Frequency on the Carlton Trail and Red River Trails

### The Pre-1885 Patrol System (1878–1884)

The NWMP did **not** maintain a formalized, scheduled patrol system on the prairie cart trails during the 1878–1884 period. The organized patrol network that later became famous — covering 1,500,000 miles annually on horseback — was introduced by Commissioner **Lawrence Herchmer** only **after 1886**, in response to rising horse theft and rural crime (Macleod, 1976, pp. 46–47; Betke, 1998, pp. 210–211).

During the 1878–1884 period, patrols were:

- **Ad hoc and mission-driven** rather than scheduled. Patrols were dispatched from forts in response to specific reports — whiskey trading, horse theft, or disturbances — rather than on a regular circuit.
- **Seasonal in nature.** Winter patrols were severely limited by weather; summer and fall saw the most activity, coinciding with the peak trading and freight season on the trails.
- **Small in scale.** Typical patrol detachments ranged from **2–6 constables** under a non-commissioned officer (sergeant or corporal), occasionally reinforced for specific operations (Atkin, 1973, pp. 135–136).

### Patrols on the Carlton Trail

The Carlton Trail — running approximately 900 miles from Fort Garry (Winnipeg) through Fort Ellice, Fort Qu'Appelle, Batoche, Fort Carlton, and on to Fort Edmonton — was the primary overland route for Métis freighters, HBC employees, and settlers (Carlton Trail article, Wikipedia; Edmonton Bulletin, July 22, 1882). NWMP presence along this trail was:

- **Concentrated at forts**, not along the trail itself. The NWMP did not maintain standing patrols along the full length of the Carlton Trail. Instead, patrols radiated outward from forts that sat on or near the trail.
- **Most visible between Fort Qu'Appelle and Fort Carlton** after 1880, when the NWMP established a detachment at Fort Carlton (leased from the HBC in the 1880s) as its main base in the Saskatchewan Valley (Fort Carlton article, Wikipedia; Atkin, 1973, p. 217).
- **Infrequent in the eastern segments** (Fort Garry to Fort Ellice), where the HBC and later Canadian authorities had a longer-established presence.

**Patrol Frequency Estimate (Carlton Trail, 1880–1884):**
- From Fort Carlton: Patrols of 2–4 men likely went out **biweekly to monthly** during the trading season (May–October), covering the trail between Duck Lake, Batoche, and the South Saskatchewan River crossing.
- From Fort Qu'Appelle: The NWMP detachment at Fort Qu'Appelle maintained a "substantial police station" and served as a center for service in the Qu'Appelle Valley and surrounding rural communities (Fort Qu'Appelle article, Wikipedia). Patrols from here covered the trail junctions south toward the CPR line and north toward the Carlton Trail.
- The NWMP "increased its presence in the area over the winter" of 1884–1885 as tensions rose (Macleod, 1976, p. 103; Atkin, 1973, p. 214).

### Patrols on the Red River Trails

The Red River Trails — a network of ox-cart routes connecting Fort Garry/Winnipeg with Saint Paul, Minnesota — had **declined significantly by 1878**. The opening of the railway between Saint Paul and Winnipeg (completed 1878 by the St. Paul, Minneapolis and Manitoba Railway) superseded the ox-cart trails for commercial transport (Red River Trails article, Wikipedia; Gilman, 1979).

- By 1878, the Red River Trails were primarily used for **local, short-distance traffic** between Métis settlements along the Red River and the international border, not the long-distance caravan trade of the 1840s–1860s.
- NWMP presence in this area was based at **Winnipeg** and **Fort Dufferin** (near Emerson, on the border). These detachments focused on **customs enforcement** — collecting duties on goods crossing the border — and on monitoring the movement of Sitting Bull's Sioux, who had fled to Canada in 1876–1877.
- Commissioner Irvine (who replaced Macleod in 1880) played a "prominent part" in negotiations with the Sioux, and the NWMP deployed **around 200 men to Fort Walsh** specifically to oversee the immigrant Sioux community (Atkin, 1973, pp. 117–123, 124).

### The NWMP Force Size and Disposition

| Year | Total NWMP Strength | Key Disposition Changes |
|------|-------------------|------------------------|
| 1878 | ~300 | HQ at Fort Walsh; forts at Macleod, Calgary, Edmonton, Carlton, Ellice, Dufferin, Swan River |
| 1880 | ~300 | Irvine replaces Macleod as Commissioner |
| 1882 | **500** (increased) | HQ moves to Regina; new forts along CPR construction |
| 1884 | ~500 | Increased presence in Saskatchewan Valley as tensions rise |
| Mar 1885 | **562** (mobilized) | Full mobilization for the North-West Rebellion |

*(Sources: Atkin, 1973, pp. 160, 172; Horrall, 1973, pp. 75–76; Macleod, 1976, p. 103)*

## 2. NWMP Authority Over Métis Travelers

### Legal Framework

The NWMP's authority derived from several overlapping legal instruments:

1. **The Mounted Police Act (1873)** — Established the NWMP with broad powers to "preserve the peace and prevent crime" in the North-West Territories. Officers were appointed as **Justices of the Peace**, giving them judicial as well as police powers (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, pp. 88, 133; Macleod, 1976, pp. 18–19).

2. **The Indian Act (1876 onward)** — Granted Indian agents broad powers over First Nations people. After 1885, agents received Justice of the Peace authority, and the pass system was administered through them (Barron, 1988; Carter, 1990, pp. 162–165).

3. **The Liquor Laws / Prohibition** — The NWMP's primary enforcement mandate in the early years (1874–mid-1880s) was suppressing the **illegal liquor trade**. Possession, sale, or transportation of alcohol in the NWT was prohibited, and the NWMP had full authority to search, seize, and arrest (Graybill, 2007, pp. 53–54; Macleod, 1976, p. 27).

4. **Customs and Excise Laws** — The NWMP collected customs duties along the U.S.-Canada border and had authority to seize undeclared or dutiable goods. Superintendent Crozier received a **bonus of $3,659 in 1886** for successful customs collections and seizures (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, pp. 19–20).

5. **The Vagrancy Act** — Used as a secondary tool to detain Indigenous people who were "off reserve" without authorization. This was the legal pretext under which the NWMP enforced the **pass system** after 1885, despite the pass system itself having no statutory basis (Barron, 1984, p. 30; Smith, 2009, pp. 60–77).

### Could the NWMP Stop, Search, and Confiscate from Métis Travelers?

**Yes, with significant qualifications:**

| Action | Legal Authority | Applied to Métis? | Notes |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|-------|
| **Stop and question** travelers on trails | General police powers under Mounted Police Act | Yes | The NWMP had broad discretion to stop anyone on trails within the NWT |
| **Search for liquor** | Prohibition laws | Yes | This was the NWMP's primary enforcement mandate; Métis cart trains were regularly searched for alcohol |
| **Search for stolen goods** (esp. horses) | Property law / criminal code | Yes | Horse theft enforcement increased after 1880 (Hubner, 1998, pp. 58–61) |
| **Collect customs duties** on cross-border goods | Customs and Excise Act | Yes, at border crossings | Particularly on the Whoop-Up Trail and at border forts (Fort Walsh, Fort Macleod) |
| **Demand passes/papers** restricting movement | **No legal basis** — the pass system was an administrative policy, not law | **Primarily First Nations, not Métis** | The pass system targeted Treaty Indians on reserves; Métis were not "status Indians" under the Indian Act and were not legally subject to the pass system (Barron, 1988; Carter, 1999, p. 195) |
| **Confiscate furs/trade goods** | Only under specific pretexts (see Section 5) | Selectively | No general authority to seize furs; confiscation required a legal pretext |

### Critical Distinction: Métis vs. First Nations Under the Law

The NWMP's authority over **Métis** travelers was **significantly more limited** than their authority over **First Nations** people:

- **Métis were not "Indians" under the Indian Act** and were not confined to reserves. They were legally free to travel, trade, and settle where they chose (Carter, 1999, p. 195).
- **The pass system did not legally apply to Métis.** It was designed for First Nations on reserves. However, in practice, NWMP officers sometimes detained Métis people under vagrancy laws or general police powers, especially during the 1885 crisis.
- **Before 1885**, the NWMP's primary interaction with Métis on the trails was through **liquor enforcement** and **customs collection**, not movement restriction.

## 3. Checkpoints, Roadblocks, and Inspection Stations on the Trails

### Formal Checkpoints

**There were no formal, permanent "checkpoints" or "roadblocks" on the Carlton Trail or Red River Trails between 1878 and 1885** in the modern sense of a staffed inspection station where all travelers were stopped.

However, the NWMP created **functional equivalents** through their fort system:

#### Forts as De Facto Checkpoints

Every major fort on or near the Carlton Trail functioned as a **de facto inspection point**. Travelers passing through or near a fort would be observed, questioned, and potentially searched. The forts were positioned at key river crossings, trail junctions, and supply depots:

| Location | Fort | Trail | Function as Checkpoint |
|----------|------|-------|----------------------|
| South Saskatchewan River crossing | Near Batoche/Fort Carlton | Carlton Trail | Major crossing point; NWMP at Fort Carlton monitored traffic |
| North Saskatchewan River | Fort Carlton | Carlton Trail | HBC post leased by NWMP in 1880s; main NWMP base in Saskatchewan Valley |
| Qu'Appelle Valley | Fort Qu'Appelle | Carlton Trail (junction) | NWMP maintained "substantial police station"; crossroads of historic trails |
| Battle River confluence | Fort Battleford | Carlton Trail (northern branch) | Capital of NWT; NWMP fort monitored trail and river traffic |
| North Saskatchewan (upstream) | Fort Edmonton | Carlton Trail (western terminus) | NWMP detachment monitored arrivals from the trail |
| Cypress Hills | Fort Walsh | Whoop-Up Trail / southern routes | NWMP HQ 1878–1882; monitored cross-border traffic |
| Oldman River | Fort Macleod | Whoop-Up Trail | Monitored whiskey trade and cross-border traffic |

#### Border Inspection Stations

The NWMP did maintain **border surveillance points**, particularly after 1882 when the force was increased to 500 men:

- **Fort Walsh** served as the primary border monitoring station from 1878–1882, overseeing the Whoop-Up Trail and the Cypress Hills region where American whiskey traders crossed into Canada.
- **Fort Macleod** monitored the southern Alberta border.
- After 1882, as the NWMP shifted focus to the CPR construction, **border patrols** continued but were supplemented by the railway's own police.

### The Klondike-Era Checkpoint Model (Not Applicable to 1878–1885)

It is worth noting that the NWMP's later **formal checkpoint system** — established during the 1897–1898 Klondike Gold Rush at mountain passes and border points, where every traveler was checked for illegal weapons, supplies, and customs duties (Berton, 2001, pp. 154, 248–249; Morrison, 1985, p. 63) — **did not exist during the 1878–1885 period**. The prairie trail enforcement model was far more informal and fort-based.

### Ad Hoc Roadblocks and Patrol Encounters

Rather than fixed checkpoints, the NWMP relied on:

1. **Patrol encounters** — Small patrols riding the trails would stop and question travelers they met. This was intermittent and unpredictable.
2. **Fort-based inspections** — When travelers arrived at or near a fort to resupply, seek shelter, or cross a river, they came under NWMP scrutiny.
3. **Seasonal operations** — During specific crackdowns (e.g., the 1874–1876 whiskey trade suppression, or the 1882 enforcement around CPR construction), the NWMP would establish **temporary detachments** at key points along trails.

## 4. Escalation Timeline: 1878–1885

### 1878–1879: Consolidation at Fort Walsh

- **Fort Walsh becomes NWMP headquarters** (1878–1882). Commissioner James Macleod commands from here. The fort oversees the Cypress Hills region, the Whoop-Up Trail, and the Sitting Bull situation.
- The NWMP deploys **~200 men to Fort Walsh** specifically to oversee the 5,600 Sioux who had crossed from the United States (Atkin, 1973, pp. 124, 147).
- Whiskey trade enforcement continues as the primary mission; the trade has been "considerably curtailed" since 1874 but persists on the Whoop-Up Trail (Whoop-Up Trail article, Wikipedia; Berry, 1995).
- Last Canadian buffalo herds eliminated by 1879; First Nations become dependent on police-issued supplies (Hubner, 1998, p. 58; Atkin, 1973, pp. 141–143).

### 1880: Leadership Change

- **Commissioner Irvine replaces Macleod** after accusations of financial mismanagement (Macleod, 1976, p. 41; Atkin, 1973, p. 215).
- Irvine is described by later historians as a **weak commissioner** whose "excessive caution" would prove costly during the rebellion (Macleod, 1976, p. 105; Horrall, 1973, p. 85).

### 1881–1882: The CPR Era Begins

- **Canadian Pacific Railway construction begins** (1881). The NWMP shifts its focus to supporting the railway — escorting construction crews, enforcing liquor laws in construction camps, and resolving labour disputes (Atkin, 1973, pp. 183–185).
- **NWMP headquarters moves from Fort Walsh to Regina** (1882), the new territorial capital near the CPR line (Atkin, 1973, pp. 164–165).
- **Force increased to 500 men** to cope with new CPR-related tasks (Atkin, 1973, pp. 160, 172; Horrall, 1973, pp. 75–76).
- **Fort Walsh is closed and dismantled in 1883** as the NWMP's center of gravity shifts east to the railway corridor (Fort Walsh article, Wikipedia; Parks Canada).
- The **CPR route is shifted south** from its originally planned path through Battleford along the Carlton Trail, to a more southerly route through Regina. This diminishes the Carlton Trail's importance but does not eliminate it (Fort Battleford article, Wikipedia).

### 1882: Whiskey Trade Crackdown

- The NWMP enforcement of prohibition intensifies as the CPR brings in more workers and settlers. **Mounted police searches, deployment of undercover officers, and large court fines** are used to suppress the liquor trade (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, p. 35; Macleod, 1976, pp. 137–139).
- On the **Whoop-Up Trail**, the whiskey trade has been largely eliminated since 1874, but legitimate goods continue to flow from Fort Benton, Montana, to Fort Macleod (Whoop-Up Trail article, Wikipedia; Berry, 1995).
- The NWMP also enforces prohibition against **white settlers**, not just First Nations, causing growing public resentment (Macleod, 1976, p. 133).

### 1883: Rising Tensions

- The NWMP is tasked with **relocating First Nations to reserves north of the railway** line, cutting them off from the U.S. border. The police make arrests and the government reduces rations; by 1883, "the threat of starvation had forced the First Nations to relocate" (Atkin, 1973, pp. 166–167).
- **Three industrial boarding schools** for First Nations adolescents established, including one at Fort Qu'Appelle (on Mission Lake near Lebret), one at Battleford, and one at High River (Fort Qu'Appelle article, Wikipedia; Stonechild, 2006).
- NWMP officers are paid **bonuses for successful customs seizures**: Superintendent Crozier receives $3,659 in bonuses (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, pp. 19–20). This creates an incentive structure that may have led to aggressive enforcement.

### 1884: Mounting Crisis

- **Dominion Land Survey** teams begin dividing the Saskatchewan Valley into the square township system, threatening Métis riverlot farms laid out in the seigneurial pattern. The 36 families of St. Louis discover the Crown has sold their farms to the Prince Albert Colonization Company (North-West Rebellion article, Wikipedia; Harris & Matthews, 1987, p. 93).
- **Métis delegation travels to Montana** to ask Louis Riel to return. Riel comes and sends an appeal to the government, receiving a "vague response" (Beal & Macleod, 1984).
- **Hayter Reed** (Assistant Indian Commissioner) and **Edgar Dewdney** (Lieutenant Governor) begin pushing for a pass system. In 1884, Vankoughnet first alludes to a pass system in his annual report, claiming it could "prevent Indians from camping indiscriminately near white settlements" (Barron, 1988).
- The NWMP is **"concerned about potential instability since the previous fall"** and increases its presence in the Saskatchewan Valley over the winter of 1884–1885 (Macleod, 1976, p. 103; Atkin, 1973, p. 214).
- In 1884 and 1885, Dewdney instructs the NWMP to **"detain native leaders as they attempted to travel to meetings, breaking up gatherings where legal resistance and court challenges were being discussed"** (Belshaw, 2016, p. 53).

### March 1885: Rebellion Erupts

- **March 18**: Riel declares a provisional government at Batoche; rebel territory carved out (North-West Rebellion article, Wikipedia).
- **March 26**: Superintendent **Leif Crozier** leads 55 NWMP and 43 civilian volunteers from Fort Carlton to seize a cache of supplies; confronts a larger rebel force at **Duck Lake**. The NWMP suffers 12 dead and 11 wounded. Five Métis and one Cree are killed (Atkin, 1973, pp. 217–220; Ouellette, 2014, p. 55).
- **March 27**: Fort Carlton is **abandoned and destroyed by fire**. The NWMP retreats to Prince Albert (Atkin, 1973, pp. 220–222).
- **Commissioner Irvine** garrisons Prince Albert with 90 police and remains there throughout the campaign, for which he is later criticized (Macleod, 1976, pp. 103–105; Atkin, 1973, pp. 238–239).
- The NWMP's **total operational strength is 562** at the start of the conflict (Macleod, 1976, p. 103).

### April–June 1885: Military Campaign

- Over **5,000 militia** under Major-General Middleton deployed west via CPR (completed just in time — the last spike driven November 7, 1885).
- NWMP abandon most posts along the North Saskatchewan Valley. Inspector **Francis Dickens** evacuates Fort Pitt by boat (Atkin, 1973, pp. 228–229).
- NWMP serve as scouts and advance guard: 74 mounted police with Otter's column at **Cut Knife**; 20 NWMP with Strange's column pursuing Big Bear (Macleod, 1976, p. 104).
- **Battle of Batoche** (May 9–12): Middleton defeats the Métis; Riel surrenders.
- NWMP casualties: 3 killed at Duck Lake (Arnold, Garrett, Gibson), 3 at Cut Knife (Corporals Sleigh and Lowry, Trumpeter Burke), 1 at Fort Pitt (Constable Cowan) (NWMP during North-West Rebellion article, Wikipedia).

### Aftermath: Post-1885 Intensification

- NWMP establishment increased to **1,000 men** "officially in order to cope with the growing white population, but also to prevent any future uprising by the Métis and First Nations" (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, pp. 3, 11).
- **The pass system is formalized** — Reed's July 20, 1885 "Memorandum on the Future Management of Indians" calls for confinement of all "rebels" to reserves, confiscation of their horses, and the pass system (Reed, 1885; Nestor, 1998).
- The pass system is "first initiated on a large scale" during the 1885 crisis and becomes "the most notorious of the post-1885 measures" (Carter, 1990, pp. 162–5).
- Commissioner Herchmer introduces the **formal patrol system** — scheduled visits, surprise "flying patrols," and outpost networks — after 1886 (Betke, 1998, pp. 210–211; Macleod, 1976, pp. 46–47).

## 5. Confiscation of Goods

### Did the NWMP Seize Furs, Trade Goods, and Weapons from Métis Travelers?

**Yes, under specific legal pretexts.** The NWMP did not have general authority to confiscate goods at random, but several enforcement frameworks gave them effective power to seize property:

### 5a. Liquor Seizures

- **The most common and legally robust basis for confiscation.** The NWMP's primary enforcement mandate was suppressing the illegal liquor trade.
- Alcohol found in possession of anyone in the NWT — whether First Nations, Métis, or white — could be seized and destroyed.
- "Many members of the force drank alcohol themselves, including liquor confiscated from smugglers" (Macleod, 1976, pp. 134–136), suggesting widespread seizures.
- **RPG Mechanic:** Any Métis cart train carrying alcohol — even a small personal supply — was subject to seizure. The NWMP conducted **undercover operations** and **surprise searches** specifically targeting the liquor trade (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, p. 35).

### 5b. Customs Seizures

- The NWMP collected customs duties at border points. Goods crossing the U.S.-Canada border without proper declaration or payment of duties could be **seized and forfeited**.
- Superintendent Crozier's $3,659 bonus in 1886 was specifically for "successfully collecting customs duties and seizing illegal goods" (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, pp. 19–20). This **financial incentive structure** encouraged aggressive enforcement.
- The Whoop-Up Trail was the primary corridor for cross-border trade; Fort Macleod and Fort Walsh were the primary customs collection points.

### 5c. Stolen Property / Horse Theft

- The NWMP increasingly targeted **horse theft** in the 1880s, driven by the Department of Indian Affairs' belief that the practice slowed assimilation (Hubner, 1998, pp. 58–61; Macleod, 1976, p. 27).
- Horses believed to be stolen — whether from white ranchers or across the U.S. border — could be seized and returned to their claimed owners.
- This enforcement sometimes targeted **Métis and First Nations travelers** on the trails, as horse stealing was "common among the First Nations on the prairies" and formed "part of intertribal competition and warfare" (Hubner, 1998, p. 58).

### 5d. Weapons Seizures

- During the **1885 rebellion**, the NWMP and militia seized weapons from captured or surrendered Métis and First Nations combatants.
- Reed's July 1885 memorandum specifically called for **confiscation of the horses of rebels** (Reed, 1885).
- **Before 1885**, there was no general authority to seize weapons from travelers. The NWMP themselves were armed with Winchester Model 1876 repeating rifles (from 1878) and Enfield revolvers (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, pp. 223–225).

### 5e. Furs and Trade Goods

- **No general authority existed to seize furs or trade goods** from Métis travelers simply because they were Métis.
- However, furs could be **indirectly targeted** through enforcement of:
  - **Customs violations** (if furs were being transported across the border without declaration)
  - **The HBC's residual trade claims** (though the HBC monopoly had been effectively broken by 1870)
  - **Vagrancy laws** (used after 1885 to restrict First Nations' commercial activity; Bourgeault, 2006, pp. 293–296)
- After 1885, the pass system and permit system **restricted First Nations' ability to sell agricultural products and goods** (Carter, 2007, pp. 117–9; Buckley, 1992, pp. 53–4). This affected Métis who lived on or near reserves more than those in the Southbranch settlements.

### 5f. Pretexts for Confiscation — Summary for RPG Use

| Pretext | Goods Targeted | Legal Basis | Frequency on Trails |
|---------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Illegal liquor | Whiskey, rum, "firewater" | Prohibition laws | **Common** — primary enforcement target |
| Customs violations | Any undeclared cross-border goods | Customs and Excise Act | **Common** at border forts; rare on interior trails |
| Horse theft | Horses, sometimes other livestock | Criminal code | **Moderate** — increasing after 1880 |
| Rebellion-related | Weapons, ammunition, horses | Martial law / military authority (1885 only) | **Intense** during March–June 1885 |
| Vagrancy / loitering | Indirect — used to restrict movement and trade | Vagrancy Act | **Post-1885** — primarily against First Nations |
| No legal basis | Any goods | **None** — but NWMP sometimes acted beyond their authority | **Rare but documented** — e.g., pass system enforcement despite being illegal |

## 6. NWMP–Métis Community Relationships

### Neither Purely Hostile Nor Purely Cooperative

The relationship between the NWMP and Métis communities between 1878 and 1885 was **complex, shifting, and regionally variable**. It cannot be reduced to simple hostility or cooperation.

### 6a. Pre-1885: Cautious Coexistence

- **The NWMP's original mandate was not directed at the Métis.** The force was created to suppress the American whiskey trade, prevent U.S. incursions, and manage First Nations relations. The Métis were not the primary target of NWMP enforcement in the 1870s.
- **Macdonald had originally wanted Métis policemen** in the NWMP, "commanded by white Canadian officers in a similar manner to the British Indian Army," but abandoned this after the 1870 Red River Rebellion "called their loyalty into question" (Macleod, 1976, pp. 8–9).
- The NWMP's approach to Indigenous peoples generally has been characterized as **"benevolent despotism"** (Atkin) or **"legal tyranny"** (Jennings) — rigidly enforcing Canadian law while occasionally being relatively supportive of Indigenous people against white ranchers' claims (Macleod, 1976, p. 27; Jennings, 1974, p. 58; Breen, 1974, pp. 115–116).

### 6b. Cooperative Moments

- **NWMP distributed relief to Métis communities** affected by the disappearance of the buffalo herds and by the 1885 rebellion's aftermath. "The police helped to distribute relief, including to the Métis communities affected by the rebellion" (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, pp. 20–21).
- **The NWMP provided emergency medical assistance** and delivered mail to remote areas (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, pp. 20–21).
- **Some First Nations leaders cooperated with the NWMP** during the 1885 rebellion, "in part the result of the good relationship the police had built up with them" (Macleod, 1976, p. 105; Atkin, 1973, p. 222). Crowfoot's Blackfoot notably did not join the rebellion.
- **Métis freighters** were the primary transport contractors on the Carlton Trail and were hired by both the HBC and the NWMP to carry supplies. This created an economic interdependence.

### 6c. Sources of Tension

- **The Dominion Land Survey** (1882) that imposed the square township system on Métis riverlot farms was not an NWMP initiative, but the NWMP was the enforcement arm of the government that carried it out.
- **Liquor enforcement** disproportionately affected Métis communities, for whom the production and trade of alcohol (especially the occasional trade of "firewater" to First Nations) was an economic activity.
- **Horse theft enforcement** targeted First Nations and Métis practices that had cultural and economic significance beyond simple criminality (Hubner, 1998, pp. 58–61).
- **The pass system** (post-1885) was enforced by the NWMP even though the force knew it was illegal and violated Treaty guarantees (Hubner, 1998, pp. 68–69). In May 1893, Commissioner Herchmer ordered the NWMP to stop enforcing it, saying it was illegal, only to be overruled by the Department of Indian Affairs.

### 6d. The 1885 Fracture

The 1885 rebellion **permanently damaged** the NWMP's relationship with Métis communities:

- The NWMP were the **first government forces** the Métis fought at Duck Lake. Crozier's attempt to seize supplies was the spark that ignited armed conflict.
- After the rebellion, the NWMP **enforced the pass system, monitored Métis communities with informants, and employed First Nations scouts** who could arrest other Indigenous people (Beahen & Horrall, 1998, pp. 60, 68–69).
- Riel was **imprisoned, tried, and hanged** at the NWMP barracks in Regina (Atkin, 1973, pp. 249–252).

## Appendix A: Key Terms for RPG Design

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Carlton Trail** | The 900-mile primary overland route from Fort Garry (Winnipeg) to Fort Edmonton, via Fort Ellice, Fort Qu'Appelle, Batoche, and Fort Carlton |
| **Red River Trails** | Network of ox-cart routes from Fort Garry to Saint Paul, Minnesota; largely superseded by railways by 1878 but still used for local traffic |
| **Whoop-Up Trail** | Wagon road from Fort Benton, Montana, to Fort Whoop-Up/Fort Macleod, Alberta; primary whiskey trade corridor |
| **Southbranch Settlements** | Métis communities along the South Saskatchewan River: Batoche, St. Laurent, St. Louis, Duck Lake, Fish Creek |
| **Pass System** | Administrative policy (1885–1941) requiring First Nations to carry written passes to leave reserves; **no legal basis**; not formally applicable to Métis |
| **Firewater** | Adulterated alcohol traded by American whiskey traders to First Nations; a key target of NWMP enforcement |
| **Red River Cart** | All-wood, hide-bound two-wheeled cart used by Métis freighters; could carry ~500–1000 lbs; "squealed like an untuned violin" |
| **Shaganappi** | Bison-hide strips used to bind and repair Red River carts |
| **Medicine Line** | The Canada–U.S. border, so called by First Nations who observed that NWMP law enforcement "protected" them from American violence |

## Appendix B: RPG Scenario Seeds

### Scenario 1: "The Patrol Encounter"
A patrol of 3–4 NWMP constables on the Carlton Trail between Fort Carlton and the South Saskatchewan crossing encounters a Métis cart train. The constables have orders to search for liquor. The carters are carrying furs south and trade goods north — but also a small keg of whiskey intended for a First Nations celebration. What happens?

### Scenario 2: "The Duck Lake Road"
March 1885. Superintendent Crozier has ordered the seizure of a supply cache near Duck Lake. A Métis freighter on the Carlton Trail must decide whether to warn the Southbranch settlements or continue to Fort Carlton to deliver supplies — knowing the NWMP may confiscate or commandeer the goods.

### Scenario 3: "The Pass"
After 1885, a Métis trader traveling from Batoche to Prince Albert is stopped by an NWMP patrol. The patrol demands to see a "pass" — even though the trader is Métis and not legally subject to the pass system. The officer knows this is legally dubious but has been instructed to "send any Indians without passes back to their reserves" (Reed, August 1885).

### Scenario 4: "The Customs Bonus"
An NWMP superintendent on the Whoop-Up Trail has a financial incentive to seize goods — he receives bonuses for successful customs collections. A Métis trader coming from Fort Benton with a load of legitimate goods has a customs declaration, but the superintendent claims it's incomplete and threatens seizure.

### Scenario 5: "The Fort as Checkpoint"
A Métis family traveling the Carlton Trail arrives at Fort Qu'Appelle to resupply. The NWMP detachment insists on "inspecting" their cart — not just for liquor but for any "suspicious" goods. The family has furs from an independent trapping venture. The NWMP officer suggests the furs might be "stolen" from HBC territory.
