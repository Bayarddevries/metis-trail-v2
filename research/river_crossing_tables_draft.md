## General River Crossing Rules

### Skill Checks

| Check | Skill | Purpose |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Crossing DC** | Survival (or Athletics for swimming) | Successfully navigate the crossing without incident |
| **Navigation DC** | Survival (or Perception to spot) | Find the best natural crossing point in the area |
| **Ice Integrity DC** | Survival | Assess ice thickness and safety (winter only) |

### Season Definitions

| Season | Months | General Conditions |
|--------|--------|--------------------|
| **Spring Break-up** | March | Ice breaking up; extremely dangerous; rivers largely impassable |
| **Spring Thaw** | April–May | Runoff swelling; high, fast, muddy water; flooding common |
| **Summer** | June–August | Normal to low water; best crossing season (late Aug can be very low) |
| **Autumn** | September–October | Falling water levels; stable; ice forming by late October |
| **Early Winter** | November | Ice forming but thin; dangerous transitional period |
| **Deep Winter** | December–February | Solid ice; crossing on ice possible but risk of frostbite and breakthrough |

### Advantage / Disadvantage Modifiers

| Condition | Modifier |
|-----------|----------|
| Experienced river guide (Métis or Cree, knows the river) | Advantage on Navigation DC |
| Oxen-drawn cart (heavier, more stable but less maneuverable) | +2 Crossing DC, −2 Navigation DC |
| Horse-drawn cart (lighter, more agile) | −2 Crossing DC, +2 Navigation DC |
| Cart train of 5+ carts (mutual aid, but also more chaos if one fails) | Advantage on Crossing DC if calm; Disadvantage if any cart in the train fails |
| Night crossing | Disadvantage on all checks |
| Rain in past 48 hours | +2 to all DCs |
| Known ford (previously scouted or marked) | Automatic success on Navigation DC; Advantage on Crossing DC |
| Flood conditions | +5 to Crossing DC, +3 to Navigation DC |

## 1. South Saskatchewan River

**Classification:** Major river — the largest waterway crossed on the trail to Batoche
**Width:** 250–400 m (main channel); multiple channels with islands common
**Depth:** 0.5–3 m at fords (varies enormously by season); 5–10 m in channels during spring
**Current:** Moderate to strong (1.5–3 m/s typical; 4+ m/s during spring thaw)

### Historical Crossing Points

| Crossing | Location | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Batoche Crossing** | Near the St. Laurent de Grandin mission, ~3 km south of Batoche settlement | Primary Métis crossing on the trail from the south; used by cart trains coming from Moose Jaw/Regina area toward the South Branch settlements. Sand/gravel bottom. Shallow riffles at normal water. |
| **Clark's Crossing** | ~15 km northwest of Batoche | Used by both Métis and later military (Middleton's column in 1885); narrower but deeper channel |
| **Rivière la Biche (Elk River) ford** | Near the mouth of the Carrot River | Secondary crossing; avoided the main channel but required a longer detour |
| **Trail Creek ford** | South of present-day Saskatoon | Used by trails approaching from the west; rocky bottom, treacherous in high water |

**Métis practice:** The Métis of the South Branch (Batoche, St. Laurent, St. Louis) maintained a network of known fords marked by willow poles and cairns on both banks. The Batoche crossing was the most reliable, with a broad gravel bar that reduced depth significantly in summer. Cart trains would wait days or even weeks for water to drop after spring thaw before attempting the crossing.

### Seasonal DC Table — South Saskatchewan River

| Season | Crossing DC | Navigation DC | Special Risks |
|--------|------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Spring Break-up** (March) | 25 | 20 | Ice breakup; massive ice floes; essentially impassable — attempt only in dire emergency. Ice can shear cart wheels; flooding of banks. |
| **Spring Thaw** (April–May) | 20 | 15 | High, muddy water; current 3–4 m/s; debris; hypothermia risk (water ~2–8°C). Gravel bars submerged. Ford impossible at most points; cart-raft required. |
| **Summer** (June–August) | 12 | 10 | Best season. Late June still high in some years; by August, gravel bars exposed and fords passable. Low water in late August: Crossing DC drops to 10. Risk: quicksand in sandy channels. |
| **Autumn** (Sept–Oct) | 13 | 10 | Stable, low water early; rising by late October with rains. Ice forming at edges by late October adds +2 DC. |
| **Early Winter** (November) | 18 | 12 | Ice forming but unreliable; shoreline ice extends 10–30 m, open water in center. Cannot ford (ice+water mix); cannot safely cross on ice. Worst season to attempt. |
| **Deep Winter** (Dec–Feb) | 15* | 12 | *Ice crossing only.* Ice typically 40–80 cm thick by January — sufficient for carts. Risk: breakthrough at pressure ridges and near springs. Frostbite check (DC 12 Con save or 1 level exhaustion) if crossing takes >1 hour in wind. |

### Special Rules — South Saskatchewan

- **Quicksand:** On any failed Crossing DC by 5+, one or more cart wheels break through a sand lens. Cart is mired. Recovery requires a DC 15 Strength (Athletics) check per wheel, or abandonment of the cart.
- **Island Channels:** The river frequently splits around islands. Each channel requires a separate Crossing DC. A Navigation DC success identifies the route with the fewest channels.
- **Batoche Ford Knowledge:** A character with the **Local Knowledge: South Branch** feature gains automatic success on Navigation DC at the Batoche crossing and Advantage on Crossing DC in summer and autumn.

### Historical Accounts

- **Gabriel Dumont** described the South Saskatchewan fords as "wide and shallow in summer, but murderous in spring" and noted that the Métis maintained crossing markers (Dumont, recounted in Steele, 1889).
- **Father Julien Bigot** of the St. Laurent mission recorded in 1882 that a cart train from Moose Jaw waited eleven days at the Batoche crossing for the river to drop after spring runoff (Bigot, "Letters from St. Laurent," 1882, Archives of the Oblates of Mary Immaculate, Ottawa).
- **General Middleton's 1885 campaign** records: The South Saskatchewan was "in flood" when Middleton's column reached Clark's Crossing in early May, requiring construction of a scow raft; several horses were nearly lost in the attempt (Denison, *Soldiers of the Queen*, 1890; Boulton, *Reminiscences of the North-West Rebellions*, 1886).
- **Peter Erasmus**, Métis interpreter and guide, described crossing the South Saskatchewan near Batoche in summer as "no great difficulty if you know the fords" but warned that the current could sweep a cart downstream if the oxen lost footing (Erasmus, *Buffalo Days and Nights*, 1976 [originally dictated 1920s]).

## 2. North Saskatchewan River

**Classification:** Major river — the gateway to the Cumberland District and the Saskatchewan country
**Width:** 200–350 m at crossing points
**Depth:** 0.5–2.5 m at fords; 4–8 m in channels during spring
**Current:** Moderate to strong (1.5–3.5 m/s); very strong during spring melt from the Rockies

### Historical Crossing Points

| Crossing | Location | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Fort Carlton Crossing** | Directly adjacent to Fort Carlton Hudson's Bay Company post | The primary crossing on the Carlton Trail. HBC maintained a cable ferry/scow from the 1820s onward. In summer, a usable ford existed on the west channel. |
| **Fort Pitt area** | Near the HBC Fort Pitt post | Downstream of Carlton; wider, shallower, but more islands and channels |
| **St. Laurent de Grandin** (upstream) | Near the Métis settlement on the North Branch | Ford used by local Métis; not on the main Carlton Trail |
| **Victoria Crossing** (downstream) | Near the old Victoria Mission (Pakan) | Used by trails toward Fort Edmonton; deeper, swifter water |

**Métis practice:** The North Saskatchewan at Fort Carlton was the most critical crossing on the entire Carlton Trail. The HBC operated a flat-bottomed scow (and later a cable ferry) here, which Métis cart trains could use for a fee — but in spring, even the ferry was often overwhelmed. When the ferry was unavailable, Métis drivers would seek fords on the west channel and raft the main channel. This was the chokepoint that controlled all traffic between the plains and the Saskatchewan country.

### Seasonal DC Table — North Saskatchewan River

| Season | Crossing DC | Navigation DC | Special Risks |
|--------|------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Spring Break-up** (March) | 25 | 20 | Massive ice drives from the Rockies; the North Saskatchewan is fed by mountain snowmelt and breaks up violently. Ice jams can raise the river 3–5 m above normal. Absolutely impassable without a major ferry operation. |
| **Spring Thaw** (April–May) | 20 | 15 | Mountain runoff arrives 2–3 weeks later than local thaw; river rises progressively through May. Current extreme (3–5 m/s). HBC ferry often non-operational. Cart-raft mandatory; even then, extremely hazardous. |
| **Summer** (June–August) | 13 | 10 | River drops steadily through June. By July, the west channel ford is typically passable. HBC ferry operational for main channel. Late August: lowest water; Crossing DC drops to 11. |
| **Autumn** (Sept–Oct) | 14 | 10 | Stable, good conditions. Water rises slightly with autumn rains. Ice at edges by late October. |
| **Early Winter** (November) | 18 | 13 | Transitional ice; the North Saskatchewan, being glacier-fed, stays open longer than prairie rivers. Shore ice unreliable. Ferry ceases operation. Dangerous. |
| **Deep Winter** (Dec–Feb) | 14* | 12 | *Ice crossing.* Typically solid by late December. River ice can be 60–100 cm. However, mountain-fed springs can create thin patches. The ice route at Carlton is well-traveled and marked. |

### Special Rules — North Saskatchewan

- **HBC Ferry at Carlton:** When operational (typically June–October, and in mild Dec–Feb on ice), the ferry allows a cart train to cross with a Crossing DC of only 8 (the ferryman does the work). However, there is a toll (2–5 shillings per cart, or trade goods equivalent). In high water, the ferry may refuse to run — check by rolling 1d6: on 1–2, ferry is not running due to conditions.
- **Mountain Runoff Lag:** The North Saskatchewan rises later than prairie rivers because its headwaters are in the Rockies. In May, even if local weather is warm and dry, the river may still be rising. Characters without Local Knowledge may be caught by surprise — impose Disadvantage on Navigation DC if they assume local conditions determine river level.
- **Cable Scow:** If the HBC ferry is unavailable, characters can attempt to rig a cable scow (DC 15 Survival to construct, requires rope, lumber, and 4+ hours). A cable scow reduces Crossing DC by 5 compared to free rafting.

### Historical Accounts

- **Isaac Cowie**, HBC clerk at Fort Carlton (1868–1873), wrote extensively about the crossing: "The North Saskatchewan at Carlton is the master crossing of the whole trail. In summer it is easy enough with the ferry; in spring it is a terror" (Cowie, *The Company of Adventurers*, 1913).
- **Milton and Cheadle** crossed the North Saskatchewan at Fort Carlton in 1862 and described the ferry operation: "The scow was dragged across by a rope stretched from bank to bank, the current doing most of the work" (Milton & Cheadle, *The North-West Passage by Land*, 1865, p. 198).
- **Father André** (parish priest at St. Laurent, North Branch) noted that Métis cart drivers would sometimes wait at Carlton for "a week or more" for the river to drop, camping on the west bank and living off pemmican stores (André, correspondence, 1883, Archives of the Archdiocese of St. Boniface).
- **Paul Kane**, the artist, crossed the North Saskatchewan near Fort Pitt in 1847 and described the current as "so strong that our horses could barely make headway" (Kane, *Wanderings of an Artist*, 1859, p. 286).
- **John Macoun**, naturalist surveying for the CPR in 1880, described the Carlton crossing in late May: "The river was still high and muddy; the ferry rope had snapped twice that spring" (Macoun, *Manitoba and the Great North-West*, 1882, p. 345).

## 3. Assiniboine River

**Classification:** Major river — crossed on routes from Fort Qu'Appelle toward Winnipeg and the Red River Settlement
**Width:** 50–120 m (narrower than the Saskatchewans but steep-banked)
**Depth:** 1–3 m at fords; 4–6 m in channels during spring
**Current:** Slow to moderate (0.5–2 m/s typical); can reach 3 m/s during spring flood

### Historical Crossing Points

| Crossing | Location | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Fort Qu'Appelle Crossing** | Near the HBC Fort Qu'Appelle (in the Qu'Appelle Valley) | Primary crossing for the Carlton Trail's eastern leg. A usable ford existed on a gravel bar below the fort. |
| **Grand Vallée (Valley) Crossing** | Near present-day Fort Qu'Appelle village | Broad shallows; used by cart trains when the fort crossing was flooded |
| **Portage la Prairie Crossing** | Near the HBC Portage la Prairie post | On the route toward the Red River; wide, shallow ford on a limestone shelf |
| **St. François Xavier Crossing** | Downstream, near the Métis parish of St. François Xavier | Well-known ford used by the Red River Métis; limestone bed, relatively stable |
| **The Forks (Winnipeg)** | Confluence of the Assiniboine and Red River | No ford; ferry or boat required. HBC and later the municipal ferry operated here. |

**Métis practice:** The Assiniboine was the river the Métis knew best, as it ran through the heart of the Red River Settlement country. The fords at St. François Xavier and Portage la Prairie were communal knowledge. Métis cart drivers from the settlement could cross these almost without thought in summer — the river was part of their daily landscape. However, the Assiniboine was notorious for its spring floods, which could inundate the entire valley floor for miles on either side.

### Seasonal DC Table — Assiniboine River

| Season | Crossing DC | Navigation DC | Special Risks |
|--------|------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Spring Break-up** (March) | 22 | 17 | Ice jams common; the Assiniboine is relatively low-gradient and prone to massive ice-jam flooding that can raise water 4–6 m. Valley floor may be inundated for 1–3 km from the channel. |
| **Spring Thaw** (April–May) | 18 | 13 | Major flooding. The Assiniboine watershed drains a huge area of eastern Saskatchewan and western Manitoba. In 1826, 1852, and 1861, catastrophic floods inundated the entire Red River colony. Valley crossings are submerged; must seek high-bank crossing points or wait. |
| **Summer** (June–August) | 10 | 8 | Easy. The Assiniboine drops to a placid, low river by late June. Fords are well-marked and well-known. By August, some fords are nearly dry. Crossing DC 8 in late August. |
| **Autumn** (Sept–Oct) | 11 | 8 | Excellent conditions. Water stable and low. Risk of sudden rise from autumn storms. Ice forming in late October. |
| **Early Winter** (November) | 15 | 10 | Ice forming; the Assiniboine is slower to freeze than prairie rivers due to its size and flow. Shore ice extends 15–40 m by mid-November. Dangerous mix. |
| **Deep Winter** (Dec–Feb) | 12* | 10 | *Ice crossing.* Reliable ice by January (30–60 cm). Well-traveled routes between St. François Xavier and Portage la Prairie. Risk: springs near the riverbed can thin ice; these are locally known. |

### Special Rules — Assiniboine

- **Valley Flooding:** During Spring Thaw, the Assiniboine Valley can flood for 1–3 km on either side. This is not just a river crossing — it becomes a **swamp crossing**. Characters must navigate standing water, mud, and floating debris for miles. Treat as an extended challenge: 3 consecutive Survival checks at DC 13, with each failure costing 1d4 hours and a random supply item lost to water damage.
- **St. François Xavier Ford:** A character with **Local Knowledge: Red River Country** can automatically find this ford and gains Advantage on Crossing DC at all times. This was the most-used ford in the entire Métis country.
- **Limestone Shelf:** The Portage la Prairie ford crosses a natural limestone shelf that provides excellent footing. Oxen and horses rarely stumble here. Crossing DC is reduced by 2 at this location.

### Historical Accounts

- **Alexander Ross** described the Assiniboine at the Forks during the 1826 flood: "The whole face of the country was one vast sea of water… the people of the colony were driven to the highest points of the settlement" (Ross, *The Red River Settlement*, 1856, pp. 98–101).
- **Father Georges Belcourt** of St. François Xavier described the local ford in an 1846 letter: "The river here is shallow and the bottom firm; our people cross with the carts at all seasons except the spring, when the Good Lord sees fit to remind us of our frailty" (Belcourt, correspondence, Archives of the Archdiocese of St. Boniface).
- **Henry Youle Hind** surveyed the Assiniboine in 1857 and noted: "At Portage la Prairie the river flows over a ledge of limestone which affords an excellent natural ford. The carts of the Hudson's Bay Company and the half-breed hunters cross here without difficulty in summer" (Hind, *Reports of Progress…*, 1858, p. 112).
- **George Bryce** recorded that the 1852 Assiniboine flood destroyed "nearly all the crops" at the Red River Settlement and that cart trains were stranded for weeks (Bryce, *The Romantic Settlement of Lord Selkirk's Colonists*, 1910, p. 148).
- **Métis oral histories** collected by the Manitoba Métis Federation note that the Assiniboine was called "the sleeping river" in summer but "the angry river" in spring (MMF, *Métis Land Use and Occupancy Study*, 1984, oral history transcripts).

## 4. Red River

**Classification:** Major river — the destination river; crossed near Winnipeg / Selkirk
**Width:** 50–100 m at crossing points
**Depth:** 1–3 m at fords; 5–10 m during spring flood; the Red is a slow, muddy river
**Current:** Very slow (0.3–1 m/s typical); the Red is one of the slowest major rivers in North America

### Historical Crossing Points

| Crossing | Location | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **The Forks (Winnipeg)** | Confluence of the Red and Assiniboine | Primary crossing point. HBC ferry operated here from the 1830s. No reliable ford — too deep and steep-banked. |
| **St. Boniface Ferry** | Downstream of the Forks, connecting St. Boniface to the west bank | Cable ferry; essential for traffic between the French/Métis parishes and the HBC post |
| **St. Vital Crossing** | South of St. Boniface | A usable ford existed here in low water; known to local Métis |
| **St. Norbert Crossing** | At the Métis parish of St. Norbert | Ford on a clay/mud bar; usable in late summer; treacherous footing (mud, not gravel) |
| **St. Andrew's Crossing** | At the Anglican parish of St. Andrew's (downstream toward Lake Winnipeg) | Shallow limestone shelf; good ford in summer |
| **Selkirk Crossing** | Near the Stone Fort (Lower Fort Garry) | HBC maintained a ferry; also a ford in low water |

**Métis practice:** The Red River was home. The Métis parishes stretched along both banks for 50 miles south of the Forks. In summer, multiple fords were available and well-known to any local person. The Red was not a dangerous river in normal conditions — it was slow, warm, and familiar. The danger was entirely in spring, when the Red's immense floodplain turned into a lake. The catastrophic floods of 1826, 1852, and 1861 are central traumas in Métis and Selkirk Settler memory.

### Seasonal DC Table — Red River

| Season | Crossing DC | Navigation DC | Special Risks |
|--------|------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Spring Break-up** (March) | 23 | 15 | Ice jams at the Forks and at bends; the Red runs north, so ice breaks up upstream first and jams against still-frozen downstream sections. Massive ice walls can form. |
| **Spring Thaw** (April–May) | 20* | 12 | *The real danger is the floodplain, not the river itself.* The Red can overflow its banks for 5–10 km on either side. In major flood years, the entire settlement is at risk. In normal years, the river is high and muddy but crossable by boat/raft (DC 15 if you can find the channel through the flooded plain). |
| **Summer** (June–August) | 8 | 5 | Trivially easy for any local. The Red drops to a slow, shallow river. Fords at St. Norbert and St. Andrew's are well-known. Crossing DC 5 reflects the fact that a stranger could still get mired in mud. |
| **Autumn** (Sept–Oct) | 9 | 5 | Excellent conditions. Same as summer with slight risk of early ice. |
| **Early Winter** (November) | 14 | 8 | Ice forming; the slow-moving Red freezes relatively quickly. Shore ice extends fast. Ferry may cease operation. Transitional hazard. |
| **Deep Winter** (Dec–Feb) | 10* | 8 | *Ice crossing.* Very reliable ice (40–70 cm). The Red River trail (winter road on ice) was a major travel route. Well-marked and well-traveled. Minimal risk except at springs and near the Forks where the Assiniboine current thins the ice. |

### Special Rules — Red River

- **Home Ground:** Any character raised in the Red River Settlement gains Advantage on all Red River Crossing and Navigation checks in summer, autumn, and winter. In spring, no one has advantage — the river doesn't care who you are.
- **Mud Hazard:** The Red River bottom is clay and silt, not gravel. On any Crossing DC failure by less than 5, a cart sinks to the axle in mud. Recovery requires DC 12 Strength (Athletics) and 1d6 × 10 minutes of digging and pushing.
- **The Great Floods:** In any year where the GM determines a major flood is occurring (historical frequency: roughly every 15–20 years, but the 1852 and 1861 floods were only 9 years apart), the Red River Valley becomes a freshwater sea. All Crossing DCs become 20+. Navigation DC becomes 18 (the channel itself is hard to find among miles of standing water). Settlements are evacuated to high ground. This is a campaign-level event, not a routine crossing.
- **North-Flowing River Hazard:** The Red River flows north into Lake Winnipeg. In spring, the upper (southern) reaches thaw first while the lower (northern) reaches remain frozen. This causes ice jams and sudden rises at the downstream end. A character making a Navigation check who fails by 5+ may misjudge where the ice jam is and be caught in a sudden rise.

### Historical Accounts

- **Alexander Ross** on the 1826 flood: "On the 5th of May the water rose suddenly… by the 6th the whole settlement was inundated. The people fled to the high lands… many lost everything" (Ross, *The Red River Settlement*, 1856, p. 99). Ross recorded that the water rose 12 feet above normal at the Forks.
- **Governor George Simpson** of the HBC wrote that the Red River ferry was "wholly inadequate" during spring floods and that the Company should invest in a proper bridge (Simpson, correspondence, 1831, HBC Archives, A.11/1).
- **Father Noël-Joseph Ritchot** of St. Norbert (the parish priest who later became a negotiator of the Manitoba Act) described the St. Norbert ford as "a matter of habit for our people; they know where the mud is deepest and where the clay is firm" (Ritchot, parish records, 1860s, Archives of the Archdiocese of St. Boniface).
- **Louis Riel** in his writings references the Red River as a constant presence in Métis life: "Our river, which the old ones call the Blood River for its color in spring, is gentle in summer and terrible in the flood" (Riel, cited in Siggins, *Riel: A Life of Revolution*, 1994, p. 34, from Riel's poetry).
- **William Hespeler**, immigration agent, described the Red River winter road in 1873: "The ice road from Emerson to the Forks is the great highway of winter; it is smooth, level, and capable of bearing any weight" (Hespeler, report to the Manitoba Department of Agriculture, 1873, Manitoba Legislative Library).

## 5. Qu'Appelle River

**Classification:** Medium river — tributary of the Assiniboine; crossed on the Carlton Trail east of Fort Qu'Appelle
**Width:** 20–50 m at crossing points
**Depth:** 0.3–1.5 m at fords; 2–4 m during spring
**Current:** Slow to moderate (0.3–1.5 m/s); the Qu'Appelle is a prairie river, relatively gentle

### Historical Crossing Points

| Crossing | Location | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Fort Qu'Appelle Crossing** | Near the HBC post at the river crossing | The trail forded the Qu'Appelle just below the fort. Broad, shallow, gravel bottom. Easy in normal conditions. |
| **Echo Valley Crossing** | In the Qu'Appelle Valley, upstream of the fort | Used as an alternate when the fort crossing was flooded; narrower but deeper |
| **Indian Head Crossing** | Near the CPR construction camp at Indian Head (1882–83) | A newer crossing developed during the railway era; flat prairie approach, good ford |
| **Cropp's Crossing** | Near the mouth of the Qu'Appelle where it joins the Assiniboine | Used by traffic heading directly to the Assiniboine without going through the fort; muddy, slower |

**Métis practice:** The Qu'Appelle was a valley crossing rather than a river crossing in many places — the river was small, but the valley was deep and steep. The challenge was often getting carts down into the valley and back up the other side, rather than crossing the water itself. Métis drivers would sometimes dismantle carts and carry them down the valley slopes by hand, reassembling on the riverbed.

### Seasonal DC Table — Qu'Appelle River

| Season | Crossing DC | Navigation DC | Special Risks |
|--------|------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Spring Break-up** (March) | 17 | 12 | Ice jams in the narrow valley; water rises fast. Valley slopes become impassable mud. |
| **Spring Thaw** (April–May) | 15 | 12 | River swells with runoff; the valley bottom floods. The steep valley walls are the real hazard — carts can slide down the muddy slopes uncontrollably. |
| **Summer** (June–August) | 8 | 5 | Easy. River is low, warm, and slow. Some fords barely wet the cart wheels. Valley grass provides good grazing while waiting. |
| **Autumn** (Sept–Oct) | 9 | 5 | Excellent. Low water. Risk: valley slopes can be slippery with frost in late October. |
| **Early Winter** (November) | 13 | 10 | Ice forming; valley access roads freeze hard (which actually helps — slopes become passable). River ice thin but forming. |
| **Deep Winter** (Dec–Feb) | 8* | 5 | *Ice crossing.* Very easy. The Qu'Appelle freezes solid. Valley access is easier on frozen ground. A well-known winter route. |

### Special Rules — Qu'Appelle

- **Valley Descent/Ascent:** The Qu'Appelle Valley is 60–100 m deep in places. Getting a cart train down into the valley and back up requires a DC 10 Survival (or Animal Handling) check for the descent and a DC 12 for the ascent (harder going up). On failure, a cart may tip or a draft animal may stumble. In spring thaw, these DCs increase to 14 and 16 respectively due to mud.
- **"The Call" (Qu'Appelle Legend):** The river's name comes from the Cree/legend of a voice calling across the valley. A GM may use this as atmosphere — but also as a mechanical hook: characters who fail a Wisdom (Perception) check by 5+ while crossing at night may become Disoriented (Disadvantage on next check) as they hear the legendary call and lose focus.

### Historical Accounts

- **James Stevens** crossed the Qu'Appelle on the Carlton Trail in 1871: "The river at the Fort is but a creek, easily forded, but the valley is deep and the descent troublesome for loaded carts" (Stevens, journal, Glenbow Archives, M-510).
- **John Macoun** noted in 1880 that the Qu'Appelle Valley was "the most beautiful stretch of country in the North-West" and that the river crossing itself was "a mere nothing in summer" (Macoun, *Manitoba and the Great North-West*, 1882, p. 156).
- **Father Hugolin Lemoine** recorded that the Métis would camp in the Qu'Appelle Valley during buffalo hunts, using the natural shelter and fresh water: "The valley is our inn on the trail" (Lemoine, cited in Payment, *Les Métis de l'Ouest canadien*, 1990, p. 87).

## 6. Souris River

**Classification:** Medium river — crossed on the trail from the Qu'Appelle / Assiniboine country south toward the Missouri trade routes
**Width:** 15–40 m at crossing points
**Depth:** 0.2–1.2 m at fords; 2–3 m during spring
**Current:** Very slow (0.2–0.8 m/s); the Souris is a meandering prairie river

### Historical Crossing Points

| Crossing | Location | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Fort Ellice Crossing** | Near the HBC Fort Ellice (at the confluence of the Souris and Assiniboine) | Primary crossing on the Carlton Trail's southern branch. Broad, shallow ford. |
| **Souris Mouth Crossing** | Where the Souris enters the Assiniboine | Used by cart trains heading to Fort Ellice from the south; gravel bar ford |
| **Hartney Crossing** | Near present-day Hartney, MB | On the route toward the Turtle Mountain and the Missouri trade; muddy ford |
| **La Souris (Mouse River) Crossing** | Near the Métis wintering camp sites in the Souris plain | Used seasonally by Métis buffalo hunters; well-known fords marked by buffalo stone markers |

**Métis practice:** The Souris (Mouse River) was a secondary obstacle compared to the Assiniboine or the Saskatchewans. It was narrow and slow. The main challenge was the surrounding prairie — the Souris Plain was flat and featureless, making Navigation DC important for finding the crossing at all. Métis hunters knew the fords by heart; strangers could ride past them without noticing.

### Seasonal DC Table — Souris River

| Season | Crossing DC | Navigation DC | Special Risks |
|--------|------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Spring Break-up** (March) | 15 | 12 | Ice jams; the Souris is slow and meandering, so ice can back up extensively. Water rises gradually. |
| **Spring Thaw** (April–May) | 14 | 10 | Flooded banks; the Souris plain is flat, so even a moderate rise inundates a wide area. The river becomes a marsh. |
| **Summer** (June–August) | 7 | 8 | Crossing is trivially easy — but Navigation DC is higher because the fords are unmarked and the river looks the same for miles. In late August, the river can nearly dry up in drought years. |
| **Autumn** (Sept–Oct) | 8 | 8 | Easy conditions. Good crossing. |
| **Early Winter** (November) | 12 | 8 | Ice forming; the Souris freezes early (shallow and slow). Ice usually safe by late November. |
| **Deep Winter** (Dec–Feb) | 7* | 6 | *Ice crossing.* Very easy. Solid ice. The Souris in winter is essentially a road. |

### Special Rules — Souris

- **Invisible Ford Problem:** The Souris fords are not visually distinctive. A stranger in the area must make a Navigation DC check to find a ford even in summer. Failure means riding along the riverbank for hours looking for a shallow spot (1d6 hours lost). Local Knowledge eliminates this requirement.
- **Drought:** In drought years (GM determination, or roll 1d6: on a 1, it's a drought year), the Souris may be nearly dry by August. Crossing DC drops to 5, but Navigation DC increases to 12 (the river channel is hard to distinguish from the surrounding prairie, and mud can trap carts).

### Historical Accounts

- **John Hind** surveyed the Souris in 1857 and described it as "a sluggish stream flowing through a level plain… easily forded in summer but spreading widely in the spring" (Hind, *Reports of Progress…*, 1858, p. 94).
- **Father Pierre-Jean De Smet**, the Jesuit missionary, crossed the Souris in 1839 and described the fords as "so shallow that our horses' hooves barely wetted" (De Smet, *Western Missions and Missionaries*, 1863, p. 202).
- **Métis buffalo hunter accounts** (collected by Garrioch, *The First Bridge*, 1937) describe the Souris plain as "easy to get lost on" and note that hunters relied on stone markers and the positions of the Turtle Mountains to navigate to the fords.

## 7. Battle River

**Classification:** Minor/medium river — tributary of the North Saskatchewan; crossed on the Carlton Trail between Fort Carlton and Battleford
**Width:** 15–30 m at crossing points
**Depth:** 0.3–1.0 m at fords; 1.5–3 m during spring
**Current:** Slow to moderate (0.5–1.5 m/s); the Battle River is small but runs through a deep, wooded valley

### Historical Crossing Points

| Crossing | Location | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Battleford Crossing** | Near the NWMP post at Battleford (established 1876) | The primary crossing on the trail between Carlton and Battleford. Broad ford on a gravel bed. NWMP maintained a ferry in later years. |
| **Eagle Hills Crossing** | South of Battleford, near the Eagle Hills | Used as an alternate route; steeper valley access but shallower water |
| **Rivière la Bataille ford** | Traditional Métis crossing south of the confluence with the North Saskatchewan | Used by Métis hunters traveling between the Battle River country and Fort Carlton |

**Métis practice:** The Battle River was a minor obstacle compared to the North Saskatchewan. Its valley was steep and wooded, providing good shelter for camping, but the river itself was small. The Métis knew the Battleford ford well and could cross it easily in any season except spring. The name "Battle River" (Rivière la Bataille / Notoki-cos-a-sibi) comes from a historical conflict between Cree and Blackfoot nations; the river's crossing was a chokepoint in that conflict, which speaks to its strategic (if not hydrological) importance.

### Seasonal DC Table — Battle River

| Season | Crossing DC | Navigation DC | Special Risks |
|--------|------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Spring Break-up** (March) | 15 | 10 | Ice breakup in the wooded valley; debris (fallen trees) is common. Valley slopes are muddy and nearly impassable. |
| **Spring Thaw** (April–May) | 14 | 8 | Swollen with runoff; valley slopes are the primary obstacle (DC 14 Animal Handling for descent/ascent with carts). River itself moderate. |
| **Summer** (June–August) | 7 | 5 | Easy. The Battle River in summer is knee-deep at the ford. Good camping in the valley. |
| **Autumn** (Sept–Oct) | 8 | 5 | Easy. Beautiful conditions; the valley is sheltered from early prairie winds. |
| **Early Winter** (November) | 11 | 5 | Ice forming quickly (small, shallow river). Usually safe by mid-November. Valley access improves as ground freezes. |
| **Deep Winter** (Dec–Feb) | 6* | 5 | *Ice crossing.* Very easy. The Battle River freezes solid and is used as a winter road in places. |

### Special Rules — Battle River

- **Valley Camp:** The Battle River valley provides excellent shelter. Any party that camps in the valley after crossing gains Advantage on the next day's Constitution saves against cold and exhaustion. The valley is 20–40 m deep and well-treed, blocking prairie wind.
- **Deadfall Hazard:** In spring and summer, fallen trees and submerged branches in the river channel can snag cart wheels. On a Crossing DC failure by 3+, a cart wheel strikes a submerged log. The wheel may break (50% chance, requiring repair — DC 12 with tools, 2 hours).

### Historical Accounts

- **Inspector James Macleod** of the NWMP described the Battle River ford near Battleford in 1876: "The river is inconsiderable in size and easily forded at all ordinary stages of water" (Macleod, NWMP report, 1876, Library and Archives Canada, RG-18).
- **Father Jean-Marie Lestanc** of the Oblates crossed the Battle River en route to Fort Carlton in 1878 and described it as "a small but pretty river in a deep valley; the crossing gave us no trouble" (Lestanc, journal, Archives of the Oblates of Mary Immaculate, Ottawa).
- **Peter Erasmus** noted that the Battle River valley was "the best camping spot between Carlton and Battleford; good water, wood, and grass, and the river an easy crossing" (Erasmus, *Buffalo Days and Nights*, 1976, p. 215).

## Cart-Raft Conversion Rules

Not all rivers require cart-raft conversion. Below is a summary of which rivers can be forded vs. which require rafting, by season.

### Ford vs. Raft Summary

| River | Can be Forded? | Cart-Raft Required? | Notes |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|-------|
| **South Saskatchewan** | Summer, Autumn (at known fords) | Spring Thaw, Early Winter | Even in summer, some channels may require rafting if the river is higher than normal. |
| **North Saskatchewan** | Summer, Autumn (west channel at Carlton) | Spring Thaw, Early Winter | HBC ferry typically available June–October. East channel at Carlton always requires ferry/raft. |
| **Assiniboine** | Summer, Autumn, Deep Winter (ice) | Spring Thaw (floodplain crossing) | Cart-raft rarely needed; fords are sufficient in normal conditions. Floodplain navigation is the real challenge in spring. |
| **Red River** | Summer, Autumn, Deep Winter (ice) | Spring Break-up, Spring Thaw (in flood years) | Ferry available at the Forks and St. Boniface. Fords adequate in summer. |
| **Qu'Appelle** | All seasons except peak spring | Never strictly required | The valley is the obstacle, not the river. Cart-raft unnecessary. |
| **Souris** | All seasons except peak spring | Never required | River is small and slow. Fords always adequate in summer. |
| **Battle** | All seasons except peak spring | Never required | Minor river. Always fordable in summer. |

### Cart-Raft Conversion Mechanics

When cart-raft conversion is necessary, the process works as follows:

1. **Preparation Time:** 2d4 hours to disassemble the cart and construct the raft. This requires:
   - The cart itself (the cart bed becomes the raft platform)
   - Rope (50 ft minimum)
   - Empty water barrels or buffalo bladders for buoyancy (2 per cart minimum)
   - Poles or paddles (improvised from willow or ash; 2 per raft)

2. **Construction Check:** DC 12 Survival (or DC 10 with a character who has proficiency in Carpenter's Tools). Failure by 5+ means the raft is unstable — Crossing DC is at Disadvantage. Failure by 10+ means the raft cannot be completed; start over.

3. **Raft Crossing:** Use the Crossing DC for the river and season, but with the following modifiers:
   - +2 DC (rafting is inherently less stable than fording)
   - −2 DC if the character has the **River Runner** feature (Métis river experience)
   - Each raft can carry one cart and its load, plus up to 4 people
   - Oxen and horses must swim alongside (Animal Handling DC 10; on failure, the animal panics and must be retrieved downstream)

4. **Reassembly:** After crossing, reassembly takes 1d4 hours and a DC 10 check (Survival or Carpenter's Tools). No check needed if the original builder is present and has proficiency.

## Failure Consequences

### Crossing DC Failure

| Margin of Failure | Consequence |
|-------------------|-------------|
| **Fail by 1–4** | **Minor incident.** Cart is swamped but recoverable. 1d3 random supply items (pemmican, flour, ammunition) are waterlogged and ruined. The crossing takes 2d6 extra hours. The party is wet, cold, and miserable. |
| **Fail by 5–9** | **Serious incident.** Cart is partially submerged and mired. One draft animal is injured (reduce speed by half until treated). 2d4 supply items are lost or ruined. One character must make a DC 12 Constitution save or gain 1 level of exhaustion (hypothermia). Cart requires DC 14 Strength (Athletics) check to extract, or must be partially unloaded (lose 25% of cargo). |
| **Fail by 10–14** | **Major incident.** Cart is swept downstream or breaks through ice. Cart is heavily damaged — cannot move until repaired (DC 15 Carpenter's Tools, 1d8 hours, requires lumber). One character goes **Down** (0 HP equivalent — unconscious, hypothermic, or submerged and rescued). Half of cargo is lost. Draft animals scattered (DC 14 Animal Handling to recover, or they flee 1d6 miles). |
| **Fail by 15+** | **Catastrophic.** Cart is destroyed (swept away, smashed by ice, or sunk). One character goes **Down** and requires immediate rescue or dies (drowning, hypothermia, or crushed by ice). All cargo is lost. Draft animals are lost or killed. This is a campaign-altering event — the party is stranded on the far bank (or the near bank, if the crossing failed early) with no cart, no supplies, and possibly a dying companion. |

### Navigation DC Failure

| Margin of Failure | Consequence |
|-------------------|-------------|
| **Fail by 1–4** | **Wrong ford.** The crossing point is worse than expected. Crossing DC increases by 2 (the water is deeper or faster at this spot than at the best ford). |
| **Fail by 5–9** | **Lost crossing.** The party cannot find a ford and spends 1d6 hours searching. Exhaustion risk (DC 10 Con save or 1 level exhaustion). Must retry Navigation DC after the delay. |
| **Fail by 10+** | **Fatal error.** The party attempts a crossing at a point that is actively dangerous — a false ford, a thin-ice area, or a steep bank collapse. The GM should impose the worst version of the Crossing DC for that season and location, plus an additional +3 DC. |

### Ice Crossing Failure (Winter Only)

| Type of Failure | Consequence |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Ice breakthrough (Crossing DC fail by 5+)** | Cart wheel or draft animal breaks through ice. DC 14 Strength (Athletics) to pull free before the ice collapses further. On failure: cart is partially submerged. Hypothermia check for anyone in the water (DC 14 Con save or 2 levels exhaustion). |
| **Ice breakthrough (Crossing DC fail by 10+)** | Major breakthrough — the cart and possibly a character fall through. This is immediately life-threatening. Character in the water has 1d4 rounds before unconsciousness from cold shock. Rescue requires DC 16 Strength (Athletics) from the bank. Cart is likely lost. |
| **Pressure ridge collapse** | On any Deep Winter crossing of a major river (Saskatchewan, Assiniboine, Red), roll 1d6: on a 1, the ice route crosses a pressure ridge. DC 15 Perception to notice in time; if unnoticed, Crossing DC is at +5 and failure means the ridge collapses under the cart. |

## Special Condition: Spring Break-Up

Spring break-up (March) is the most dangerous season on every river. The ice is breaking, the water is rising, and the entire river ecosystem is in chaos. Special rules apply:

1. **No Ford Is Reliable:** All Navigation DCs assume the fords from other seasons are still valid. In break-up, they are not. Subtract any ford-related bonuses. Assume the character is looking for any possible crossing.
2. **Ice Floes:** The river is choked with moving ice. Any crossing attempt must deal with ice floes — on each round of a crossing, there is a 30% chance of an ice floe striking the raft/cart. DC 12 Dexterity (or Strength, for a cart driver) to avoid. On failure, the floe deals 2d6 bludgeoning damage to the cart and may capsize a raft.
3. **Hypothermia:** Water temperature is 0–4°C. Any character entering the water must make a DC 15 Constitution save or gain 2 levels of exhaustion immediately. Failure by 5+: the character is incapacitated by cold shock and begins to drown.
4. **No One Crosses in Break-Up:** Historically, virtually no one attempted a river crossing during spring break-up. The Métis, the HBC, and the NWMP all halted travel during this 2–4 week period. If a party insists on crossing, the GM should make the DCs as punishing as the tables indicate and ensure the players understand the likely consequences.

## Special Condition: Flood Years

Certain years saw catastrophic flooding, particularly on the Red and Assiniboine rivers. Historical flood years in the 1878–1885 period include:

- **1879:** Moderate Assiniboine flood
- **1882:** Significant Red River flood (water 15 ft above normal at Pembina)
- **1883:** Localized flooding on the South Saskatchewan (heavy mountain snowpack)

During a declared flood year:

- All Spring Thaw Crossing DCs increase by +3
- All Spring Thaw Navigation DCs increase by +2
- The floodplain extends 2–10× normal width
- The river may not return to normal levels until late June or even July
- **Campaign Impact:** Floods destroy crops, delay trade, and strand cart trains. A flood year should have ripple effects throughout the campaign economy (scarce supplies, inflated prices, delayed shipments).

## Design Notes (for GMs and Playtesters)

1. **The DCs are calibrated for a party with moderate Survival proficiency.** A solo traveler with no Survival skill should find the major rivers in spring nearly impossible (as they were). A party with a Métis guide should find summer crossings routine (as they were). The tables aim to reflect historical reality, not game balance.

2. **The "Deep Winter is easier than Spring" pattern is intentional.** Historical accounts consistently describe winter river crossings as routine and spring crossings as terrifying. The ice was thick, the route was known, and the cold was a manageable risk. Spring flooding was not manageable — it was an act of God.

3. **The Assiniboine and Red are easy in summer because they were.** These were home rivers for the Métis. Making them difficult in summer would be historically inaccurate. The drama comes from the spring floods and the economic consequences of being stranded.

4. **Cart-raft conversion is deliberately cumbersome.** The Métis avoided it when possible, using fords instead. The rules should encourage players to wait for low water, scout for fords, and hire local guides — just as the historical cart drivers did.

5. **Failure consequences escalate deliberately.** A near-miss should be a story beat (wet pemmican, a cold night). A catastrophic failure should be a campaign event (lost cart, dying companion, stranded party). The gap between "minor incident" and "catastrophic" is meant to create genuine fear at the table when a player rolls poorly on a major river in spring.

6. **Spring Break-Up deserves special caution.** The GM should feel empowered to say "you cannot cross this river right now" — not by forbidding the attempt, but by making the DCs and consequences communicate the reality. If a party insists on crossing the South Saskatchewan during break-up, they should face DC 25 with catastrophic failure on any miss by 5+. This is historically accurate.
