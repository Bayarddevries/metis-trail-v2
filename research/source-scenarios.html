Game Structure Review

The current Metis Trail V2 engine supports:

  • Terrain types: plains, river_valley, wooded, uplands, river
  • Event system: terrain-pooled events with D20+mod vs DC rolls, choice-based outcomes, effects on food/wear/crew/morale/time
  • Settlement actions: rest, trade, repair, grease, forage, recruit, rumours, heal (varies by node type: HBC, Metis, mission, NWMP, trading, river)
  • State tracked: food, squeal (cart noise), wear, morale, crew state (rested/tired/exhausted), season, day count
  • Scoring: trade units, food remaining, crew state, daysPenalty, wearPenalty

Events are triggered by terrain-based probability rolls during travel. Each event has 2+ choices with DCs, required items, and outcome effects. The engine is ready for populated event data — it needs sourced narrative content.

Current event pool only has generic placeholder events. The following compendium provides historically-sourced scenarios ready to be formalized into engine event objects with full source citations.

I. Travel & Camp Scenarios

1. The Noisy Cart — Squeal Mechanics

Brehaut, "The Red River Cart and Trails" (MHS Transactions) mechanicalplainsvalley
"When loaded it gave forth a blood-curdling squeal which could be heard for miles and which came to be associated with it. This squeal was caused by the friction of the dry wood of the hub of the wheel turning on the axle and could not be eliminated. If grease was used the dust of the trail would mix with the grease and wear down the axle or congel and prevent the wheel from turning." — Harry Baker Brehaut, "The Red River Cart and Trails: The Fur Trade," MHS Transactions, Series 3, No. 28 (1971-72)
Game integration: This is the canonical explanation for the existing squeal mechanic. Event: a player who forgets to grease at a settlement (costs 1 shaganappi) risks squeal buildup. Event text could quote: "Your cart's axle screams across the prairie — a sound that carries for miles." Choices: (a) Apply shaganappi grease [need: shaganappi] — squeal reset, (b) Ignore it — squeal +20, attracting attention.

2. The Daily Camp — Canadian Friend's Journey (1860)

John C. Schultz, "The Old Crow Wing Trail" (MHS Transactions, 1894) travelsocial
"Two Red River ponies, who disdained oats and had never eaten of aught save prairie grass... Blackie having a bad eye and uncertain temper, with a disposition to smash things with his hind legs... the swamp must be deep that he could not pull a cart through and the bank of a stream just forded must have been steep and slippery indeed that Blackie's unshod feet could not scramble up. Bichon, the patient, would do his best and, failing, would lie down in the one or slide back to the bottom of the other." — John C. Schultz, "The Old Crow Wing Trail," MHS Transactions, Series 1, No. 45 (1894)
Game integration: Horse/ox as named companion with personality traits (brave/timid, stubborn/obedient). Travel event: "Your ox [name] balks at a steep riverbank." Choice with animal handling DC. Blackie-type failures cause wear; Bichon-type cooperation grants bonus progress.

3. The Proper Way to Ride a Cart

Schultz, "The Old Crow Wing Trail" travelmechanical
"Attempt to ride in it in any way that one is wont to do in a 'civilized vehicle, and it soon rattled... its occupant, who found himself, to a musical accompaniment of frying pan and tin kettles, trying alternately to preserve himself from being pitched onto the pony, having his right or left ribs cracked against the side rail, or turning a somersault over the tailboard of the cart. No, there is only one way to ride in a cart with ease and pleasure, and that is seated in front on its floor, with your legs hanging down near the horse's tail." — Schultz (1894)
Game integration: Minor morale event. "You try to ride sitting on the cart railing." Fail DC 10: morale -5, +1 wear. Success: "Sitting on the floor with your legs dangling, you ride in peace."

4. Stowing the Outfit — Camp Equipment Management

Schultz, "The Old Crow Wing Trail" travel
"Put into the cart by a green hand, [the axe] becomes an engine of destruction; cuts into your packages of tea, etc., ruins your blankets and jolts along till its long handle reaches far over the tail board, and an extra jump tumbles it on to the trail... The axe should take no risks, and must have a leather socket for its head and a strap for its handle, and both outside the cart on one of the side boards. The gun is the next in importance; and for that, too, there is only one way... The butt must rest near your seat on the left side, the barrels in a loop to the top rail at an angle of 45 degrees... Not so dangerous as the two former, but infinitely more difficult to manage are the frying-pan, with its long handle, and the copper and tin kettles... only when inside one another and lashed securely below the centre of the axle, where they may jingle in peace, are they to be circumvented." — Schultz (1894)
Game integration: Setup/preparation event at Fort Garry. "How do you stow the travel outfit?" Good choices (proper axe/gun/kettle storage) = morale bonus. Greenhorn choice = chance of losing item or +1 wear. Tutorial moment.

5. Camp Cookery — Rubaboo and Re-chaud

William G. Fonseca, "On the St. Paul Trail in the Sixties" (MHS Transactions, 1900) socialtravel
"After some hours of steady traveling, as the sun stood high in the sky, the welcome stop took place. This was made by some stream or lake. The oxen and horses let loose from their burden, bounded away to the water, into which they plunged neck deep, remaining there safe from the tormenting flies and mosquitoes, until hunger drove them to the pasture awaiting them. The voyageurs at once struck their camp... the Red River bannock was in course of preparation. It was simply flour, water and salt. The dough was kneaded on a bag spread out on a buffalo skin, the cakes were flattened and baked in a frying pan over the fire... the pemmican bag was broached, a quantity of it was stirred into the boiling water, flour and salt were added, and thus resulted the celebrated 'rubaboo'... Hot bannocks and piping hot 'rubaboo' were served around, the latter in cups, and the tea in tin cups soon began to disappear among the hungry company." — William G. Fonseca, "On the St. Paul Trail in the Sixties," MHS Transactions, Series 1, No. 56 (1900)
"Instead of rubaboo, 're-chaud' was served, commonly corrupted 'row-scho,' from the Latin re and French chaud, to heat over. Pemmican cooked in a frying-pan, a little grease, pepper, salt, with a trace of onions and potatoes added, constituted this, a dish to set before a king." — Fonseca (1900)
Game integration: Daily camp/rest event. "The fire crackles. Rubaboo simmers in the kettle." Resting action adds standard morale +15 (already in engine). Special variant "Cook re-chaud" if player has onions/potatoes in inventory → morale +20, crew → rested.

6. Night Camp — Music and the Red River Jig

Fonseca, "On the St. Paul Trail in the Sixties" social
"If the night was clear, and the moon flooded the prairie with her silver light, robes were spread. The sound of the fiddle invited the dance. The Red River jig was struck up, and one after another exercised himself to his heart's content, as the shouts of the audience stimulated him. Amidst peals of laughter and snatches of voyageurs' song dull care was forever banished from the camping ground... Wrapped in robes and blankets each sought to put himself at rest. The music of the spheres was rudely silenced by an appalling outburst of nasal energy, a pandemonium of discordant notes fills the air — happily 'sweet oblivion' came to the most wakeful." — Fonseca (1900)
Game integration: Evening camp morale event. "The fiddle strikes up the Red River Jig." Choice: (a) Dance — morale +10, crew → rested; (b) Sleep early — morale +5. If expedition has been on trail >5 days without rest, add: "But your crew is too exhausted to dance."

7. The Midday Camp — Capturing the Refractory Animals

Fonseca travel
"When the camp was struck the capture of oxen and ponies was always exciting. Knowing their advantage, they played a good game of hide and seek, and were coy to the advances of their masters. Sometimes to drive the refractory animals among the carts was a last resort. At such times the hot nature of the voyageur was apt to get the better of him." — Fonseca (1900)
Game integration: Random midday event. "The oxen have scattered after drinking." DC 12 crew check: success = normal restart; failure = +1 hour lost (advance clock), morale -5.

8. Travel Rations — Pemmican, Bannock, and Tea

Schultz, "The Old Crow Wing Trail" travel
"As prairie chicken and duck were abundant, the substantials for the trip were as follows: Pemmican (marrowfat if possible) 20 pounds, hard biscuit, 30 pounds, tea, sugar, butter and salt; a little flour, to make the 'Rubbiboo' assume a bulky appearance when Indians had to be breakfasted or dined, their mid-day entertainment being generally avoided by giving them a biscuit each, and keeping on ourselves with a lunch of pemmican 'au naturel;' a pair of blankets each, a couple of buffalo robes, then costing 12 shillings sterling each." — Schultz (1894)
Game integration: Starting inventory / provisioning choices. Marrowfat pemmican (premium food item) vs standard pemmican — 20% better morale recovery. Hard biscuit as backup. This could be a pre-departure event at Fort Garry.

II. Cart & Equipment Failures

9. The Broken Axle — Shaganappi and Field Repair

Fonseca, "On the St. Paul Trail in the Sixties" mechanical
"To the greenhorn on the plains, or as the Indian calls him, 'Moonias,' such a disaster seems without remedy. But the skilful voyageur soon spliced his broken trams, replaced his broken railways, and this all done by the ready use of shagganappe, so that the uninjured ox or pony was soon on his way again." — Fonseca (1900)
"Five or six of these axles are used up in the course of a trip. They are manufactured as they are needed on the way." — Fonseca (1900)
"The old cart at Lower Fort Garry National Historic Park has eleven spokes. The old cart in the Manitoba Museum of Man and Nature has twelve... twelve spokes were the most prevalent." — Brehaut (1971-72)
Game integration: Direct wear event. "The cart shudders — a crack SPLITS from the axle." Repair requires: shaganappi (1 used) + tool kit OR improvised repair with shaganappi only (-1 wear, 50% chance fails again next day). Without shaganappi: wagon immobilized, lose 1 full day cutting wood for new axle (+1 time, morale -10).

10. The Cart-Raft — Improvised Hide Boat for River Crossings

Fonseca mechanicalhazard
"Four cart wheels were taken and placed dish upwards and the four points of contact securely fastened together. On the outer rims four pieces of wood were lashed, forming a square. Meanwhile six buffalo hides were soaked, when sufficiently soft sewed together, and spread out, upon which the frame work was placed. The edges were brought up and laced to the outer bars, one line fastened to the stern, another at the bow. A party would then swim across, carrying the bow line over; the boat was launched, and floated like a duck, with a capacity of 800 lbs." — Fonseca (1900)
"The 'dished' wheel... was used as a raft when crossing rivers, by removing the wheels and lashing them together concave side upward. Then the cart body and cargo were placed upon them and ferried across the water." — Brehaut (1971-72)
Game integration: River crossing event when use-craft option exists. "The river is too deep to ford with a loaded cart." Choices: (a) Build cart-raft [need: 4 hides] — safe crossing, +1 day; (b) Ford with cart — DC 15 swim check (dangerous); (c) Wait for water to drop — +1 day safe.

11. Rawhide Tire Repair on the Trail

Brehaut mechanical
"A rawhide tire was used in some instances on the wheel of the cart. It ran all the way around on the rim of felloe of the wheel on the tread and folded over on the sides, and tied with thongs between the spokes. It was put on 'green' or wet and when it dried shrank and held in place so tightly, that it never came off but lasted as long as the cart held together." — Brehaut (1971-72)
Game integration: Spare bison hides can be used as wheel tires when applied wet. Event: "A wheel rim loosens on a rocky stretch." Use bison hide (1) → permanent repair, no wear. Without: +1 wear per day until repaired at settlement.

III. River Crossings & Water Hazards

12. Crossing the Red Lake River — The Horse-Shoe Ford

Schultz, "The Old Crow Wing Trail" hazardriver
"The carts had indeed entered straight into the water at the foot of the sloping bank we had descended, but, once in, they had turned upstream to make the crossing in a horse shoe fashion which brought them out directly on the opposite side, where again a sloping bank formed the best path for ascent and descent." — Schultz (1894)
"Searching back to the top of the bank we could find no diverging track to another part of the river, and yet it was clearly a case of swim to cross it here... it was while essaying this that I found the secret of the ford." — Schultz (1894)
Game integration: River crossing puzzle event. "The river looks too deep to ford where the tracks enter." Players who push straight in: danger (DC 15 — ox loses footing, cart flips, lose 1 food/wear +2). Players who scout upstream first (spend 1 hour): discover the horse-shoe path → safe crossing DC 8. Teaches observation.

13. Hauling Carts Down Steep Banks

Fonseca hazardvalley
"Coming to the steep bank of a river to be crossed, a line was tied to the middle of the axle of the cart, and a turn of the line made around the trunk of a tree on the bank. Thus the ox and cart was led gradually down the deep decline until the water was reached. On the opposite bank corresponding arrangements were made to haul them up from the bed of the stream." — Fonseca (1900)
Game integration: River crossing event option. "The bank is steep — 8 feet of loose earth down to the water." Choice (a) Tie to tree and lower carefully [need: rope] — safe. Choice (b) Free descent — DC 14, fail = +2 wear, cart damage.

14. Cart Upset in a Ravine or Slough

Fonseca hazard
"It happens, quite often, going over prairie trails, crossing ravines or sloughs, that cart, load, or ox, may be overturned." — Fonseca (1900)
"Being run over by the heavy wooden wheels of a Red River cart was the leading cause of death on the trails." — Cart Trail Game Research (2025), citing historical sources
Game integration: Hazard event on plains terrain. "A hidden rut drops one wheel into the creek bed — the cart tilts dangerously." DC 12 to correct; failure = cart upset, lose 1 random food item +1 wear. DC 18 = ox injured temporarily (crew → tired).

15. The Rope Brigade — Team River Crossing

Schultz travelriver "One part of the equipment of a number of carts in a brigade was a long and strong rope for river crossings and soft places... at such places, if not very bad, the rope was attached to each cart as it came up and five or six of the men at the further end aided the struggling ox or horse just at the right moment; but when the bog or slough was very bad indeed, then the animals were taken out to find their own way over, while the whole force of brigade men pulled the loaded cart through." — Schultz (1894)
Game integration: Rope as tool. "The bog swallows the cart to the hubs." Choices: (a) Unhitch ox, men pull together [need: rope] — success DC 10, safe; (b) Push through without rope — DC 16, fail = +2 wear.

IV. Social Encounters

16. Métis Hospitality — Welcome at a River Parish

Schultz socialvalley
"The traveller who brought the latest news and could speak a little French was always sure of the best they had in the way of bed and board at any of the houses of the Metis, whose settlement extended then half way to Pembina. One's horses too were always included in the generous hospitality, and Blackie and Bichon ate of the sweetest of the recently mown prairie grass." — Schultz (1894)
Game integration: Automatic morale +10 and food +2 when stopping at Métis parish nodes (St. Boniface, St. Norbert, St. François Xavier). Connection to journal/news mechanic — if player carries "news," bonus is doubled.

17. Meeting Joseph Howe on the Trail

Fonseca socialtrade
"We were just ready for lunch when a democrat wagon hove in sight containing a coal-black Sambo as driver, and three gentlemen... a distinguished party, consisting of Hon. Joseph Howe, Secretary of State for the Dominion of Canada... I invited the party to lunch with me; fortunately we had bear steak, and pemmican in its two-fold messes... I carried a bottle of very old St. Croix rum, so far as I was concerned for the stomach's sake, not the palate. At the sight of the amber fluid the Hon. Mr. Howe clapped his hands, and turning to Mr. Sanford, exclaimed, 'Sanford, there is corn in Egypt.'" — Fonseca (1900)
Game integration: Rare plains encounter event. "A democrat wagon approaches bearing men in dark suits and a black driver." Choice: (a) Offer lunch — morale +15, random trade item +1, crew impressed; (b) Continue past. Notable historical moment (1869 context).

18. Rounds of Handshakes — Métis Politeness

Fonseca social
"They presented an imposing scene on the lonely prairie... I recognized Le Pierrie, Pierre Lavallee and others, all picked men. Alighting from the democrat, the transformed son of Aesculapius doing the same, I met with a gracious reception, which was acknowledged by the process of clasping and shaking twenty hands. As I approached, each man lifted his cap. There is an innate characteristic politeness about these Metis which the rude nomadic life they lead cannot eradicate." — Fonseca (1900)
Game integration: Métis encounter events always include handshaking / greeting mechanic. Courteous approach: +morale. Showing fear or hostility: failed meeting, possible consequences.

19. The Disguise — Passing Through the Barricade (1869)

Fonseca socialconflict
"This is no time for jokes, sir; action, action. I am sorry to announce that your much-cherished Dundrearys must be sacrificed to the god of necessity... Those black, wavy, silken, flowing, magnificent whiskers, the pride of manhood, the object of careful shaping and culture, the flag nature had stuck to the mast, had now to be lowered. The tonsorial operation began, and perceptibly a moisture gathered in the corners of the victim's eyes... The Capote is brought into requisition. It fits well. Cap, belt and moccasins are all arranged: The metamorphosis is complete." — Fonseca (2869 return trip through Riel's barricade at St. Norbert)
Game integration: Special node event at St. Norbert / Fort Garry area (1869 timeline). The barricade! "You spot a line of armed men across the road." Choices: (a) Disguise and greet with handshakes [need: capote + moccasins] — DC 12 social, pass through; (b) Try to force through — DC 18 combat, likely failure + consequences; (c) Turn back to Pembina — safe but +2 days.

VI. Weather & Environmental Hazards

24. Prairie Fire

Cart Trail Game Research compilation hazardplains
"Pr Lacombe... 'The prairie burned every afternoon... the oxen grew restless in the smoke.'" — Father Albert Lacombe, Missionary Journals (1878), via GitHub metis-research-wiki
Game integration: Autumn plains event. "Smoke on the horizon. The dry grass crackles — a wall of flame races toward you." DC 14 survival check to find a firebreak (burnt ground, river, rock outcrop). Fail = lose all food stores + crew → exhausted + morale -20. Success = outflank the fire, minor food loss.

25. The Crosswinds — Windfall Blocking the Trail

Current engine events (enhanced) hazardwooded
Game integration: Wooded terrain event. "A great elm has fallen across the trail — branches splayed like fingers blocking the path." Choices: (a) Cut through [need: axe] — +2 hours (time), but gain 1 firewood bundle; (b) Bypass through the bush — DC 12, fail = +1 wear (cart scratches); (c) Wait and backtrack — +1 day.

26. Sand and Heat —Burnt Prairie Crossing

Schultz hazardplains
"Blackie and Bichon were eating, as if for a wager, of the rushes and rich grass above the sand line on its shore... our ponies chose to walk in the shallow water to cool their unshod feet, sorely tired by our hasty crossing of many leagues of burnt prairie to reach where grass could again be had." — Schultz (1894)
Game integration: Summer plains event. "The prairie here has been burnt black. No grass, no water, and the sun is relentless." Food -2, crew → tired. Choice: (a) Push through — extra day, crew → exhausted; (b) Detour to water source — +1 day but find grass and water.

27. Sand Hill Difficulties — Stumps and Coulees

Schultz hazardplains
"Many a worn-out axle and broken wheel attest the power of its stumps and coulees to make the spring and fall brigades of loaded carts look well to their gearing before entering upon this most difficult part of the trail." — Schultz (1894), describing the Crow Wing Trail sand hills
Game integration: Terrain modifier event. "The ground turns treacherous — old stumps hidden in tall grass, narrow coulees cutting across the path." DC 13 navigation; fail = +2 wear. Players with a guide/scout: auto-success.

VII. Health & Morale

28. Cholera on the Trail

Cart Trail Game Research compilation hazard
"Cholera was the most devastating threat, often striking healthy individuals in the morning and killing them by nightfall... A waterborne bacteria that thrived in the stagnant water of common camping grounds." — Cart Trail Game Research (2025), citing historical sources
Game integration: Rare but severe event, particularly at river valley camps. "A member of the crew wakes shaking and cold — by midday they cannot stand." Contaminated camp water. DC 14 with medicine pouch = recovery after 1 day rest. Without medicine: crew → exhausted for 3 days, morale -30. Teaches camp placement (avoid stagnant water).

29. Scurvy — The Winter Vitamin Deficiency

Cart Trail Game Research compilation hazard
"Scurvy: A constant risk during winter or when the bison herds could not be found, leading to a dangerous lack of Vitamin C." — Cart Trail Game Research (2025)
Game integration: Late-season event (after early winter sets in). "Your gums bleed. Your teeth feel loose. The lack of fresh food is taking its toll." If food < 5 and season = winter: morale -25 per day. Berry pemmican or Saskatoon preserve prevents/cures this.

30. Mosquitoes and Flies at Camp

Fonseca, Schultz hazard
"The oxen and horses let loose from their burden, bounded away to the water, into which they plunged neck deep, remaining there safe from the tormenting flies and mosquitoes, until hunger drove them to the pasture awaiting them." — Fonseca (1900)
"It was during this work one heard untranslatable language, as accident and adventure took place at the crossing [of the river], amidst a cloud of mosquitoes, sand flies, and all prairie annoyances, including mud." — Fonseca (1900)
Game integration: Camp event at river valley nodes. "The mosquitoes rise in clouds from the riverbank." Rest quality reduced by 1 level (rested → no rest, tired → stays tired). Night camp on high ground: normal rest. Tarp/canvas tarpaulin gives partial protection (+5 morale for comfort but still exposed).

VIII. The Buffalo Hunt

31. The Laws of the Prairie — Hunt Governance

Brehaut, citing Alexander Ross (1840 hunt) socialplains
"The rules and regulations for the hunt. On this occasion they were:
1. No buffalo to be 'run' on the Sabbath day.
2. No party to fork off, lag behind or go before, without permission.
3. No person or party to run buffalo before the general order.
4. Every captain, with his man in turn to patrol the camp and keep guard.
5. For the first trespass against these laws, the offender to have his saddle and bridle cut up.
6. In the second offence, the coat to be taken off the offender's back and cut up.
7. For the third offence the offender to be flogged.
8. Any person convicted of theft, even to the value of a sinew, to be brought to the middle of the camp and the crier to call out his or her name three times, adding the word 'thief' each time." — Harry Baker Brehaut, citing Alexander Ross's account of the 1840 hunt, MHS Transactions (1971-72)
Game integration: Event triggered on plains in summer. "You encounter a massive hunt camp — hundreds of carts in a great circle, the air thick with dust and the sound of running horses." Players can join the hunt or bypass. Joining: "Four hundred mounted huntsmen await the signal... the earth trembles." Significant food reward (pemmican +5 to +15 depending on roll) but Sunday restriction means time lost.

32. Joining the Hunt — The Circular Camp

Brehaut / Ross social
"The camp was circular in form and occupied as much area as would a city of that time. The carts were placed in a circle, side by side, with trams outward... The circle of carts served the double purpose of securing the people and animals within, as well as a place of shelter and defence against an attack from without." — Brehaut, citing Ross (1840)
Game integration: The circle of carts is both tactical and social. Event: "The Métis captain asks you to add your cart to the defensive circle." Join = morale +20, food +5 from shared hunt. Refuse = possible hostility. If Sioux are rumored in the area, the circle becomes critical: players outside the circle face a DC 18 danger event.

33. Métis Women and Pemmican Production

Cart Trail Game Research compilation trade
"The women followed in carts to butcher the carcasses and begin the production of pemmican. Once a bison was downed, the hunter marked it with a 'token' (a glove or knife) and moved to the next animal. Pemmican — a mixture of dried, pounded meat, melted fat, and berries — was a nutritional miracle. It was calorie-dense, portable, and resistant to spoilage." — Cart Trail Game Research (2025)
"A finer sort of this article, called 'berry pemmican,' was made by mixing the flesh with the berries of the saskatoon... This was considered a delicacy." — Fonseca (1900)
Game integration: Pemmican production event at hunt or when bison are encountered. Two types: standard pemmican (food +5, preserves forever) and berry pemmican (food +8, morale +5 bonus). Berry variant requires Saskatoon berries (found at river valley nodes in season).

34. The Market Exchange — St. Paul Trade

Fonseca, Cart Trail Game Research trade
"The carts carried from 800 to 1,000 lbs... at a cost of two pounds sterling, one became the possessor of a vehicle Bison Robe: 1.25 MB (or $1.25)... Pemmican (per lb): 14¢ (in 1878)... Muzzle-loading Gun: 25 MB... 3-Point Blanket: $2.44 (Approx. 2 MB)... Sugar (per lb): 0.5 MB... Beaver... the very foundation of the northern trade." — Cart Trail Game Research (2025), citing historical exchange tables
Game integration: The Made Beaver (MB) economy. Trade values should be carefully tuned. Key cart-load for independent Métis freighters: buffalo robes outbound, manufactured goods (gun, blanket, tea, sugar, rum) inbound. 20 robes = 1 gun. Players choosing trade route need to balance weight vs value.

IX. Governance, Culture & Laws

35. Gabriel's Crossing — The Ferry and the Sign

Gabriel's Crossing entity (GitHub wiki) trade
"In 1871, Gabriel Dumont settled on a farm on the east bank of the South Saskatchewan River at another Carlton Trail river crossing about six miles upstream from Batoche... He posted an advertising sign on the Carlton Trail where it divides near the present day community of Humboldt. The sign listed his rates and was written in English, French and Cree. Dumont's ferry was 22 feet long by twelve feet wide, held by rope and moved by oars. In 1877, the Northwest Territories began to regulate ferry services and Gabriel had to apply for a license to operate." — Gabriel's Crossing entity, sourced from saskatchewan-metis-communities.md (GitHub metis-research-wiki)
Game integration: Gabriel's Crossing node (already in engine). Event text should feature the multilingual sign. Choice: "Take Dumont's ferry (fair price, French/Cree/English sign) — safe crossing, lose a small fee" vs "Ford the South Saskatchewan" — DC 16, dangerous. Reflects Dumont's entrepreneurial character.

36. Fort Carlton — Major HBC Depot

X. Recommended Additional Sources

1. "The Diary of Alexander Henry the Younger" (1800-1803)

Why: Henry's journal at Pembina Post is the PRIMARY source for the first recorded Red River carts — he documents their construction and first use in exact detail. The 1801 entry about solid wheels "sawed off from the ends of logs" directly contradicts the popular myth that carts always had spoked wheels. This could be the basis for a pre-event or founding-of-cart mechanic at Pembina.

Access: Published by various academic presses. Available in the Champlain Society series.

2. "York Boats, Scows and Sturgeon-Heads" — Métis Museum (metismuseum.ca)

Why: The Métis Museum has extensive primary-source documents on York boat construction, wages, and crew life including actual HBC wage books. Essential for any York boat brigade events or Portage La Loche content.

Access: Direct PDF link

3. "The Red River Cart and Trails" — Lawrence Barkwell (Gabriel Dumont Institute)

Why: Barkwell's work is the most comprehensive single compilation of cart construction details, trail routes, and Métis transportation history. Already partially used but the full GDI publication has additional detail not in the MHS article.

4. "Homeland to Hinterland: The Changing Worlds of the Red River Métis" — Gerhard J. Ens (1996)

Why: Academic study of Métis seasonal migration patterns. Would provide the statistical backbone for modeling when and where hunting, trading, and freighting happened through the year. Critical for seasonal event timing.

5. "One of the Family: Métis Culture in the Fur Trade" — Brenda Macdougall (2010)

Why: Focuses on kinship networks and social structure. Would inform HOW Métis communities interacted with travelers — who shares food, who charges for what, how disputes were settled. Essential for encounter events at Métis nodes.

6. Alexander Ross, "The Red River Settlement" (1856)

Why: Ross provides the most detailed contemporary account of the 1840 buffalo hunt including exact numbers, the Laws of the Prairie, and camp organization. Already cited by Brehaut but should be sourced directly for game event text.

7. Manitoba Historical Society Transactions — Full Archive

Why: mhs.mb.ca/docs/transactions/ — Contains dozens of first-person accounts from trail travelers including unpublished diaries. Key issues to mine: Series 1 (Schultz, Fonseca already used), Series 3 (Brehaut already used). Also check for Carlton Trail and buffalo hunt specific articles.

8. Parks Canada — York Boats at Lower Fort Garry

Why: Living history site with full-sized York boat reproductions and experienced interpreters. Source for accurate construction details, crew sizes, sailing techniques, portage procedures. For any York boat or portage events in the game.

Access: parks.canada.ca/lhn-nhs/mb/fortgarry/culture/york

9. Library and Archives Canada — HBC Post Journals (Fort Carlton, Fort Pitt, Fort Edmonton)

Why: Daily journal entries from specific forts along the Carlton Trail. Food prices, weather, events, arrivals/departures of cart brigades — the actual day-to-day texture of life at each node. This is the ultimate source for making each settlement node feel unique.

Access: bac-lac.gc.ca HBC archives

10. "Métis Trails of Western Canada" — Encyclopédie du patrimoine culturel de l'Amérique française

Why: Detailed trail descriptions with coordinates. Would support accurate map/node placement.

Access: ameriquefrancaise.org

Source Citation Convention

Every event carries a Source object with quote, author, work, year, and URL. For the in-game UI, display the author and year as a tooltip or footnote. For the research/archive view, show the full citation and link to the original. No event should be implemented without a source object — if the source is secondary (Brehaut, Cart Trail Game Research compilation), cite the secondary source AND include the primary citation it references.

French/Michif Text

Several sources contain French text (Fonseca, the barricade verses described at St. Norbert in 1869). The game should use both English and French text where historically appropriate — particularly for Métis nodes. Michif words could be sprinkled in with tooltips. Sources: Fonseca's account of the barricade verses ("in the style of the Marseillaise"), Gabriel's Crossing sign (English/French/Cree), the word "rubaboo" (French "roux" + Ojibwe), "shaganappi" (Cree).
