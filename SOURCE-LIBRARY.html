Source Documents

Three primary first-hand accounts of life on the Red River and Carlton trails. Each has been read and tagged for game-relevant content across all systems.

1

Red River Settlement: Its Rise, Progress, and Present State — Alexander Ross (1856)

89,177 words · Narrative history · Settler on the ground
"An isolated spot in the wilds of North America, distant 700 miles from the nearest sea-port, and that port blockaded by solid ice for ten months in the year."
KEY CONTENT: Buffalo hunt logistics (1840), cart construction, Sunday camp rituals, trail distances, settlement descriptions, river crossings, seasonal rhythms
2

To Red River and Beyond — Anonymous Traveller (1875)

290,330 words · Diary/Journal · Overland expedition member
"Our expedition, on the afternoon of the 10th of June, left the hill back of the apostolic capital of Minnesota... Crowds of citizens from St. Paul and vicinity were present during the ceremony."
KEY CONTENT: Day-by-day trail narrative, mule behavior, elk/antelope hunting, mosquito swamps, Alkaline water, Mouse River junction, Assiniboine crossing, prairie fires, sandhill cranes, eagle encounter, Sunday theology lectures
3

Red River Cart and Trails: The Fur Trade — Harry Baker Brehaut (1972)

89,177 words · Historical research · Synthesizes primary accounts
"When loaded it gave forth a blood-curdling squeal which could be heard for miles... No metal of any kind was used in its construction."
KEY CONTENT: Cart construction (wheels, axles, dished design, shaganappi repairs), trail brigade organization (10 carts, 3 drivers, overseer), harness design, river crossing methods (cajeux, wheel rafts), complete trail network map with 18 named routes
4

La Vérité sur la Question Métisse — Adolphe Ouimet / Gabriel Dumont narrative (1885)

803,018 words · Political history + first-hand battle account · Dumont's own words
"J'ai fait placer un corps de garde vis-à-vis l'église de Batoche, pour empêcher l'équipage de débarquer... un câble en fer, jeté en travers de la rivière, devait faire chavirer ce bateau."
KEY CONTENT: Dumont's journey to retrieve Riel, Batoche battle (4-day siege), river blockade tactics (cable across river to capsize boats), Gatling gun, mannequin decoys, NWMP/spy encounters, ammunition crisis, Métis governance structure, land claims

🛺 Cart & Vehicle

CartMechanic
"When loaded it gave forth a blood-curdling squeal which could be heard for miles and which came to be associated with it. This squeal was caused by the friction of the dry wood of the hub of the wheel turning on the axle and could not be eliminated. If grease was used the dust of the trail would mix with the grease and wear down the axle or congeal and prevent the wheel from turning."
— Brehaut (1972), after Alexander Henry's 1801 account
New mechanic: Cart Squeal. Ungreased wheels = audible detection range increases (NWMP, buffalo, other travellers). Grease reduces squeal but adds wear. Repairs required every 3 segments. Audio cue + UI indicator.
CartMechanic
"The dished wheel was an important development in the cart. The dish was made by inclining the spokes outward from the cart body. The amount of the dish varied but was about 3 inches. It gave the cart better stability by preventing tipping on rough terrain and facilitated getting out when stuck in the mud."
— Brehaut (1972)
Cart upgrade: Dished wheels. +1 stability on rough terrain. Unlocked by finding a wheelwright at certain settlements or having a skilled crew member.
Cart
"No metal of any kind was used in its construction. Only the simplest tools were used to build it — hand-axe, small saw, draw-knife and screw-auger... The weight of the cart was approximately 550 pounds and the load capacity in the neighbourhood of 500 pounds."
— Brehaut (1972)
Cart construction: All wood and hide. Weight 550 lbs, capacity 500 lbs. Tools required: axe, saw, auger. Repairs possible on-trail with basic materials.
CartRepair
"When the dowel connecting two sections of the felloe broke, two flat pieces of wood were cut to conform to the wheel curvature, and one piece was placed on each side of the felloe at the joint requiring repair and bound together with strips of animal hide, wound crosswise around the felloe, usually referred to as shaganappe, which had been soaked in water. When the strips dried they held the curved flat wooden boards firmly against the felloe."
— Brehaut (1972)
Emergency repair: Felloe binding. Requires 1 shaganappi. Temporary — lasts 1 segment. Description text for repair event.
CartRiver Crossing
"The dished wheel was also used as a raft when crossing rivers, by removing the wheels and lashing them together concave side upward. Then the cart body and cargo were placed upon them and ferried across the water."
— Brehaut (1972)
River crossing event: Wheel-raft option. Requires removing 2 wheels (1 hour). Temporary — must reattach after crossing. Alternative: cajeux (ferry), ford, or bridge.

🐴 Animals

Mule
"Essaying to remount Mule, into whom must have transmigrated the crazy soul of some defunct geometer, he suddenly seemed to behold in me his centre, conceived himself a radius, and proposed to pass the rest of his life in describing a complimentary circumference, his tail doing the tangents. Whirling away a half hour thus... I made a desperate leap for his back, caught one toe in the stirrup, and so began a half-mile gallop, outdoing circus Mazeppas. In time, this became tedious, and I jumped off, lighting on all-fours... Then, for six or eight miles, spur, bit, and I fought Mule, his heels and his knees, and helped him conquer them."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Mule behavior event: "The Geometry Lesson." Mule refuses, spins, bucks rider off. DC 12 Horsemanship to remount. Failure = lost time + crew amusement (morale +1 for watching, -1 for the rider).
Mule
"Poor brute! on our return he fell sick. We dragged him along behind the cart for a day or two, and since he got no better, but only worse, and could hardly walk, we left him on the open prairie, cutting a heap of green grass for his bed and board, clipping his ear for a property-mark, and praying that the wolves might spare him. Good old mule! you served us well, and I couldn't help choking at the throat as I caught the last glimpse of your long neck stretched out as you lay there, 'loth to believe that we would desert you."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Animal death/leaving event: Sick animal left behind. Crew morale -2. Emotional weight. The mule clipping for property mark is a detail that could be a mechanic — marked animals can be reclaimed later.
ElkHunt
"But the prospect of killing an elk was no more to be resisted than the glimpse of office flashed upon a hopeless nominee; and so half a dozen of us capped our rifles and cantered along in the track of his great leaps, faintly hoping to surround him in some of the poplar clumps, till we saw him shake his antlers proudly and plunge into an alder swamp two or three miles away, after which we cantered back again."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Hunt event: Elk sighted. Chase sequence. Elk escapes into alder swamp. Decision: pursue (risk time/wear) or let go. Reward if pursued: +4-6 food.
Eagle
"A large gray eagle came sailing along the air, and hovered high above us. I fired with my rifle and hit him, knocking out a few tail-feathers; but not fatally... he only tumbled, fluttering three or four times his own wing-spread, and then, as if more scared than hurt, recovered himself and flew off into upper air. Afterward we saw him hanging over the river; a strong breeze was blowing but, without an apparent stroke of his pinions, he kept himself steadily poised and balanced in the same spot, head bent looking downward."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Encounter: Eagle hunt. DC 12 to wound, DC 18 to kill. Wounded eagle: morale +1 (impressive shot). Killed: +1 food + feathers (trade item / morale).
AntelopeHunt
"Here, too, after a long chase and considerable 'circumvention,' we shot at the first antelopes seen by us. Their quick, long jumps took them out of rifle-range too soon to give us a second chance."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Hunt event: Antelope. Very fast, hard to approach. DC 14 to get within range. Requires stalking (costs +1 day). Success: +3 food.
Sandhill Crane
"Sandhill cranes — huge birds, delicious to eat, and worth creeping a hundred rods to shoot — would start from many hollows as we came up over the nearest hill, and we could see their ungainly majesties putting on airs and stalking about on the top of distant sand-hills, taking care to fly before we were within rifle-shot, and mocking us with their clanging cry till their white, van-like wings were faint white specks in the distant air."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Hunt event: Sandhill cranes. Need to creep 100 rods (~550 yards). DC 12 Stealth. Reward: +3 food. Flavor about the clanging cry and "unmajestic majesties."

🌿 Environment & Trail

EnvironmentNew Event
"Our mosquito and gnat miseries culminated. Alkaline water in the swamps by which we had camped ruined the flavor of our tea, and gave all our horses and mules what Joseph called 'an elementary canal enlargement.'"
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Event: Mosquito Siege + Alkaline Water. Combined environmental hazard. Reduced tea morale bonus. Mules get sick if camped near swamp >1 night.
Landscape
"We crossed a valley called by Michelle, our guide, La Belle Vallée. Its appearance was like the deserted channel of a beautiful river, such as the Upper Mississippi would be if its waters had passed away and seas of long green grass filled their place."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Trail location: La Belle Vallée. Flavor text when crossing this stretch of trail. Unique landscape description.
Landscape
"From the last of a range of high hills... we caught sight, for the first time, of the faint blue line in the northern horizon which marked the course of the Assiniboine... Between were innumerable lakes — some salt and some fresh — shallow ones fringed with green or black rushes, and deep ones wooded to the banks."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Trail event: First sighting of the Assiniboine from the high hills. Milestone moment. Landscape detail for map/flavor.
Prairie FireNew Event
"Away to the right was a column of smoke, where the careless dropping of a match had set the prairie on fire."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Event: Prairie Fire. Smoke visible for miles. Decision: flee (lose time), burn a firebreak (costs firewood + 1 day), or wait and hope wind shifts. Risk: cart caught in fire = major wear + lost supplies.

⚔️ NWMP & Encounters

NWMPNew Event
"Un autre espion anglais passe. — D'où venez-vous, dit Dumont ? — Il n'est pas nécessaire que je vous le dise. — Êtes-vous avec nous ? — Je ne veux pas me mettre dans de mauvais draps."
— Ouimet, after Gabriel Dumont's account (1885)
Event: Spy/Informer encounter on the trail. Dumont interrogates a passing English spy who won't say where he's from. Decision: detain, follow, or let go. Sets tension for the 1885 timeline.
NWMPCrew
"dit un jour en présence de Dumont: Je sais que Gabriel a été trompé, et qu'il se fait mener. — Je n'ai pas d'instruction, c'est vrai, réplique celui-ci, mais j'ai en moi un gang que vous n'avez pas, ce qui vous empêche d'agir comme vous le devriez, vous, Métis."
— Ouimet / Dumont dialogue (1885)
Crew character: Gabriel Dumont personality. Pragmatic, skeptical of Riel's spiritual leadership, but loyal. "I have something you don't" — a gang/spine. Use for crew banter and decision moments.

🏕️ Settlement & Social

CampMorale
"We used to rest in camp one day in seven, Sunday the day, as often as was possible. Then our trousers and morals were mended, or, at least, patched up to appear a little better, the emigrants greased afresh their cart-wheels and their good resolutions, and washed away their sweat in the nearest river or lake. The man of science divided his time between Paul's Epistles and the compound microscope... Joseph and I indulged in a theological disputation, and all of us ended the day generally by gathering about the camp-fire after supper and singing Old Hundred, Balerma, Dundee, Ward."
— "To Red River and Beyond" (1875)
Sunday Rest mechanic. Every 7th day: mend clothes (repair), grease wheels (remove squeal), wash, crew activities (theology, reading, science), evening hymn singing (+2 morale). Camp scene with specific hymns named.
Buffalo HuntMajor Event
"On June 15, 1840, the carts emerged from all parts of the Settlement... The picturesque procession stretched some five or six miles... There were sixteen hundred and thirty persons and twelve hundred and ten Red River carts in this expedition... a total of 1,089,000 pounds of meat were obtained on this hunt."
— Ross, "Red River Settlement" (1856)
Major event: The Buffalo Hunt. Multi-day expedition. 1,630 people, 1,210 carts. Council of captains, rules (no hunting Sabbath, no straying, penalties: saddle cut, coat cut, flogging, public shaming for theft). Massive food reward (+50-100 food). Risk: injury, lost time.
Governance
"First a council for the nomination of officers was held which included: ten captains, each with ten soldiers under his command; ten guides and a chaplain. There was a camp flag which regulated the making and breaking of camp... During the march the flag was flying and while it was up, the guide was the chief of the expedition and everyone was subject to his orders."
— Ross, "Red River Settlement" (1856)
Brigade organization: 10 carts per brigade, 3 drivers, overseer. Camp flag system. Sunday penalties for infractions (1st: saddle cut, 2nd: coat cut, 3rd: flogging, theft: public shaming — name called 3x + "thief").
